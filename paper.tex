\begin{abstract}
Classical Rademacher and information-theoretic generalisation bounds
characterise the worst-case behaviour of supervised learners via upper
bounds on the excess risk.  However, none of these approaches
characterise the irreducible floor below which no learner can go.  We
prove a universal lower bound on the expected leave-node-out
generalisation error of any feature-restricted learner on any
graph-supervised regression problem with bounded target signal
$\|\by\|_\infty \le M$.  The bound depends only on the graph Laplacian,
the target signal, and the reachable feature subspace of the learner;
it is independent of the regressor choice and exact in expectation,
with no concentration slack.  An empirical-risk-minimising learner
saturates the bound to a slack of order $M^2/\sqrt{N - n}$ in the
proportional regime $n = \alpha N$ via the transductive Rademacher
complexity of \citet{ElYanivPechyony2006}, and this slack is
minimax-tight in rate under a dispersion assumption on the
orthogonal complement of the hypothesis class and a
leverage-incoherence condition on the class itself: no estimator
can drive the held-out error lower with probability bounded away
from zero.  The pair of bounds is the
Cram\'er--Rao analog of the classical efficiency inequality for
parameter estimation.  Three corollaries instantiate the framework:
the encoding-gap bound for materials-property prediction, a
spectral-disjointness bound on negative transfer, and an empirical
validation across the eight-task MatBench panel of the
\texttt{cycling-data-lab} organisation, on which the headline rank
correlation is Spearman $\rho = +1.000$ ($n=8$, exact permutation
$p = 4.96 \times 10^{-5}$).
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} graph-supervised learning; generalisation
lower bound; leave-node-out cross-validation; spectral methods; Laplacian
eigenfunctions; Berry--Esseen anticoncentration; Cram\'er--Rao analog;
applicability domain; transfer learning; active learning.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Generalisation bounds for supervised learning come, with rare exceptions,
in two methodological lanes.  The Vapnik--Chervonenkis (VC) / Rademacher
lane \citep{Vapnik1998,Mohri2018book,Tripuraneni2020} bounds the gap
between empirical and population risk in terms of the hypothesis class's
combinatorial or geometric complexity, yielding \emph{upper bounds} on
the excess risk.  The information-theoretic lane
\citep{Russo2016,Xu2017,Tran2019,Nguyen2023} bounds the same gap in terms
of the mutual information between the training data and the learned
hypothesis, again yielding upper bounds.  However, none of these
approaches characterise an irreducible floor below which no learner can
go: the resulting inequalities tell us how \emph{badly} a learner could
perform, not how well any learner \emph{must} perform.

In a parallel development at the intersection of graph signal processing
\citep{Shuman2013,Ortega2018} and statistical learning, the question of
\emph{leave-node-out generalisation} on a graph-supported regression
problem has acquired particular significance.  The materials-informatics
community has investigated the \emph{applicability-domain problem}
\citep{Tropsha2010,Sahigara2012,Hanser2016} --- the empirical observation
that models trained on one family of compositions generalise poorly to
materials outside that family --- through largely empirical means.  The
recommender-systems community has investigated the \emph{cold-start
problem} \citep{Schein2002,Marlin2003,Kluver2014} as a structurally
similar question on user--item graphs.  Urban mobility research has
investigated the failure of mode-choice models trained in one city to
generalise to others \citep{Naik2014,Hillel2021}.  In every case the
empirical phenomenology is unambiguous, but a quantitative theorem
characterising the \emph{floor} of the held-out error --- as a function
of the data, not of the regressor --- was, until recently, missing.

The companion paper \citet{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound} provided one
such floor, specifically for the materials-informatics setting.  The
present paper generalises that result to a universal statement that
applies to any graph-supervised regression problem under leave-node-out
evaluation, and shows that the saturating estimator is in principle any
empirical-risk minimiser whose hypothesis class subsumes the population-risk
minimiser on the relevant feature subspace.  Together with a matching
Berry--Esseen minimax lower bound, this yields a Cram\'er--Rao-style
efficiency statement for graph-supervised learning.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*),leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Universal lower bound
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}).}  For any learner $\Acal$
    with reachable hypothesis class $\Hcal_d$, target $\by \in \R^N$
    with $\|\by\|_\infty \leq M$, and uniform leave-node-out protocol
    $\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ with $|T| = n$, the expected leave-node-out loss
    satisfies
    \begin{equation*}
        \E_T\!\big[L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))\big] \;\geq\; \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)\big) \cdot \Var(\by),
    \end{equation*}
    where $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by) := 1 - L_V(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})/\Var(\by)$
    and $f^\star_{\Hcal_d} := \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d} L_V(f)$.  The
    bound is \emph{exact in expectation}: no concentration slack
    appears.  Proved as
    Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} in
    Section~\ref{sec:lower-bound-proof}.

\item \textbf{Universal upper bound
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}).}  Any empirical-risk-minimising
    (ERM) learner with truncated $L$-Lipschitz hypothesis class $\Hcal_d$ of
    transductive Rademacher complexity $\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\Hcal_d)$
    \citep{ElYanivPechyony2006} satisfies, with probability $\geq 1 -
    \eta$ over $T \sim \Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$,
    \begin{equation*}
        L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))
        \;\leq\; \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)\big) \cdot \Var(\by)
            + 2 \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\ell \circ \Hcal_d)
            + 20.2\, M^2 \sqrt{\tfrac{(n + u)\log(2/\eta)}{n u}},
    \end{equation*}
    where $u := N - n$ is the held-out cardinality and $\ell$ is the
    truncated square loss (bounded in $[0, 4M^2]$, which carries the
    $4M^2$ factor inside the constant $20.2 = 5.05 \times 4$).  The slack is $O(M^2/\sqrt{N - n})$,
    matching the Berry--Esseen lower bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}
    in rate.  Proved in Section~\ref{sec:upper-bound-proof}.

\item \textbf{Minimax tightness (Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}).}
    No estimator --- including an oracle with full population knowledge
    of $\by$ --- can drive the slack of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}
    below $\Omega(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$ with probability bounded away from
    zero, in the worst case over signals.  The argument is a
    Berry--Esseen \emph{anti-concentration} on the residual energy
    $\|\by^\star_\perp\|^2_{T^c}$, the fluctuation of which is intrinsic
    to the random test set, not estimator-dependent.  Since the
    fluctuation is centred, no high-probability one-sided lower bound
    can hold for a positive threshold; the constant-probability
    statement at $c' \ge 1/10$ is the correct asymptotic regime,
    matching the rate of the upper bound at $\Theta(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$.
    Proved in Section~\ref{sec:minimax-proof}.

\item \textbf{Cram\'er--Rao analogy.}  Theorems
    \ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}--\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound} are
    the graph-supervised-learning analog of the classical efficiency
    inequality $\Var(\hat\theta) \geq 1/I(\theta)$, where $I(\theta)$
    is the Fisher information of $\theta$:
    Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} provides a structural
    floor on the expected loss depending only on (graph, target,
    learner's reach), and Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}
    exhibits an explicit efficient estimator achieving the floor up to a
    minimax-tight slack.  The structural role of the Fisher information
    is played by the variance of the residual energy
    $((\bP_{\Hcal_d^\perp}\by)(v))^2$ on $V$.

\item \textbf{Corollaries.}
    \begin{itemize}[label=$\bullet$,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{C1 (encoding-gap recovery; \S\ref{subsec:c1}).}  The
        bound of \citet{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound} on the categorical
        vs.\ compositional encoding gap under leave-composition-out
        evaluation is a one-paragraph corollary, with the categorical
        learner the degenerate case $\Hcal_d = \{$constants$\}$ and
        the compositional learner the case $\Hcal_d = \Scal_{\mathrm{comp}}$.
    \item \textbf{C2 (negative transfer; \S\ref{subsec:c2}).}  Taking
        $V = V_{\mathrm{src}} \cup V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ and $\Pi$ the
        leave-task-out protocol, the bound recovers a structural
        \emph{spectral-disjointness} criterion for negative transfer:
        transfer is unavoidably harmful when the source and target
        labels project onto disjoint bands of the joint graph's
        Laplacian eigenbasis.
    \item \textbf{C3 (active learning; \S\ref{subsec:c3}).}  Letting the
        protocol $\Pi$ be chosen by the learner yields an
        importance-weighted variant in which the optimal sampling
        distribution is the leverage-score distribution on the learner's
        reachable subspace, with the bound matching the rate of the
        spectral-graph A-optimal design literature
        \citep{DrineasMahoney2018,Avron2017}.
    \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Empirical validation.}  The companion repositories
    \texttt{materials-applicability-bound}
    \citep{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound} and
    \texttt{mobility-applicability-bound} (Foss\'e and Pallares, in
    preparation) provide cross-domain empirical instantiations on the
    8-task MatBench panel, 27-network bike-share demand panel, and
    34{,}858-commune French mobility panel respectively.
    Section~\ref{sec:empirics} summarises the headline results and
    points to the per-repository scripts for reproduction.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Relation to prior work}
\label{subsec:related-work}

The four nearest methodological lanes are:

\textbf{(a) Rademacher / VC bounds.}  Classical generalisation bounds
\citep{Vapnik1998,Mohri2018book} and the modern transfer-learning
extensions \citep{Tripuraneni2020,Du2020} give \emph{upper} bounds on
excess risk via Rademacher complexity, eluder dimension, or task
diversity.  Our Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} reuses the
Rademacher technology to prove its upper bound, but the principal
contribution is the matching lower bound and the resulting
Cram\'er--Rao-style efficiency statement, which has no analog in the
VC lane.

\textbf{(b) Information-theoretic bounds.}  Recent
information-theoretic bounds on transferability
\citep{Tran2019,Nguyen2023} and on generalisation
\citep{Russo2016,Xu2017} provide model-free upper bounds via conditional
entropy or probably-approximately-correct (PAC)-Bayes posteriors.  Our
framework is also model-free, but in the spectral lane rather than the
information-theoretic lane, and provides lower rather than upper bounds.

\textbf{(b$'$) Leave-one-out PAC-Bayes.}  The leave-one-out PAC-Bayes
literature \citep{LangfordCaruana2002,DziugaiteRoy2017} provides
\emph{upper} bounds on the leave-one-out (and by extension
leave-node-out) excess risk for \emph{stochastic} predictors via
posterior--prior divergence.  Our framework is complementary: it
provides a \emph{lower} bound on the leave-node-out expected loss for
\emph{deterministic} ERM-on-projection predictors, characterising the
irreducible floor that the PAC-Bayes upper bounds aspire toward.  The
two lanes are largely non-overlapping in technique (transductive
Rademacher + Berry--Esseen vs.\ posterior--prior KL) and complementary
in direction (lower vs.\ upper).

\textbf{(c) Graph signal processing on bandlimited subspaces.}  The
classical bandlimited reconstruction theorems of
\citet{Pesenson2008,AnisGaddeOrtega2016} and the recent generalisation
to arbitrary feature subspaces by \citet{ChepuriLeus2018,TanakaEldar2020}
provide the underlying sampling-theoretic machinery for graph signals
restricted to a subspace.  Our framework borrows the notion of a
subspace-restricted estimator but, crucially, does \emph{not} require
the Pesenson sampling quality $\sigma_T(\Hcal_d)$ to appear in the
bound; see Remark~\ref{rem:no-sigma-T-needed} for the resolution of an
intermediate misconception in our own development.

\textbf{(d) Cycling-data-lab structural-bounds program.}  This paper is
the foundational entry of the cycling-data-lab structural-bounds
research program.  It absorbs and unifies the empirical
applicability-domain bound of \citet{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound}
(\emph{Machine Learning: Science and Technology}, materials informatics)
and the mobility-transfer instantiation in preparation by the same
authors (\emph{Transportation Research Part B}, urban mobility).  The bound
established here is the \emph{floor}; the empirical companions
demonstrate that the floor is operationally tight in two unrelated
domains.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Graph, signal, and spectrum}

Let $G = (V, E)$ be a finite undirected graph with $|V| = N$.  We treat
$V$ as the set of supervised examples and $G$ as a similarity graph
chosen by the modeller: in materials informatics, a composition-similarity
$k$-NN graph; in urban mobility, a commune-similarity graph; in
recommender systems, a user--user (or item--item) similarity graph.

The symmetric-normalised Laplacian is
$\Lsym = \bI - \bD^{-1/2} \bW \bD^{-1/2}$, where $\bW$ is the
weighted adjacency matrix and $\bD$ the diagonal degree matrix.  Its
eigendecomposition is $\Lsym = \bU \boldsymbol{\Lambda} \bU^\top$ with
$0 = \lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \cdots \leq \lambda_N \leq 2$ and
$\bU = [\bu_1, \ldots, \bu_N]$ orthonormal.

The target signal is a vector $\by \in \R^N$, treated as deterministic
(not random).  Without loss of generality we assume $\by$ is centred,
$\bone^\top \by = 0$ (this absorbs any constant predictor into the
reachable subspace; see Remark~\ref{rem:centring}).  Under this
convention, the population variance
$\Var(\by) := N^{-1} \|\by\|^2$.
We assume the signal is bounded: $\|\by\|_\infty \leq M < \infty$.

The graph Fourier coefficients of $\by$ are $\hat y_k := \bu_k^\top \by$,
so $\by = \sum_k \hat y_k \bu_k$ and $\|\by\|^2 = \sum_k \hat y_k^2$
by Parseval.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}
\label{subsec:setup-protocol}

\begin{definition}[Evaluation protocol]
\label{def:protocol}
An \emph{evaluation protocol} is a distribution $\Dcal_\Pi$ over
ordered pairs $(T, T^c)$ with $T \subset V$, $|T| = n$,
$T^c := V \setminus T$, $|T^c| = N - n$.  The canonical instance
$\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ samples $T$ uniformly without replacement.
\end{definition}

\paragraph{On the term ``leave-node-out''.} Throughout this paper,
\emph{leave-node-out} (LSO) refers to the fractional hold-out
protocol $\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ above with $\alpha = n/N$ fixed in
$(0, 1)$, in the sense of A\ref{a:proportional} (introduced in
\S\ref{sec:setup}). This encompasses random hold-out splits
(typically $\alpha = 0.8$), $k$-fold cross-validation
($\alpha = 1 - 1/k$ for $k \in \{2, 3, 5, 10, 20\}$), and
GroupKFold protocols (as used in the empirical panel of
\S\ref{sec:empirics}). The strict leave-one-out protocol ($n = N -
1$, $\alpha = (N-1)/N \to 1$) is recovered as $k \to N$ but lies
at the boundary of A\ref{a:proportional}; consequently, the
upper-bound and minimax results of
Theorems~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}--\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}
are formally stated for the interior of $(0, 1)$, with the
LOOCV special case treated separately in
Lemma~\ref{lem:cat-baseline} (whose exact form survives the
$\alpha \to 1$ boundary).

For a predictor $\hat f : V \to \R$ (identified with its vector of
node predictions in $\R^N$), we write
$L_T(\hat f) := n^{-1} \sum_{v \in T} (\hat f(v) - y_v)^2$,
$L_{T^c}(\hat f) := (N-n)^{-1} \sum_{v \in T^c} (\hat f(v) - y_v)^2$,
and $L_V(\hat f) := N^{-1} \sum_{v \in V} (\hat f(v) - y_v)^2$.
The \emph{transduction factor} is $\rho(\Pi) := N/(N-n)$; for the
standard 80/20 split, $\rho = 5$.

\subsection{Learner and reachable hypothesis class}
\label{subsec:setup-learner}

\begin{definition}[Learner]
\label{def:learner}
A \emph{learner} is a (possibly randomised) map $\Acal : (T, \by|_T)
\mapsto \hat f_\Acal(T) \in \Hcal_d$, where $\Hcal_d \subseteq \R^N$ is
the \emph{hypothesis class}: the set of vector-valued predictors the
algorithm can output, identified with their restrictions to $V$.  We
assume $\Hcal_d$ is closed under truncation to $[-M, M]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Learner-specific spectral $R^2$]
\label{def:learner-rspec}
Let $f^\star_{\Hcal_d} := \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d} L_V(f)$ be the
\emph{population-risk minimiser} of $\Hcal_d$ (which exists by
compactness under truncation).  The \emph{learner-specific spectral
$R^2$} of $\Hcal_d$ on $\by$ is
\begin{equation}
    \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by) \;:=\; 1 \,-\, \frac{L_V(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})}{\Var(\by)} \;\in\; [0, 1].
    \label{eq:rspec-learner-defn}
\end{equation}
For closed linear $\Hcal_d$, $f^\star_{\Hcal_d} = \bP_{\Hcal_d}\by$ and
$\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by) = \|\bP_{\Hcal_d}\by\|^2/\|\by\|^2$, the classical
projection $R^2$.  For non-linear $\Hcal_d$ (gradient-boosted trees,
neural networks with weight constraints, $k$-NN with local averaging),
$\Rspec$ is the population-variance-explained ceiling of the class.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Examples of $\Hcal_d$ and $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)$]
\label{rem:examples}
\begin{itemize}[label=$\bullet$,leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Categorical (one-hot) learner.}  $\Hcal_d = \{$constants on $T^c\}$
    (the learner sees one-hot indicators for $v \in T$ only, and is
    forced to a constant prediction on $T^c$).  Then
    $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by) = 0$ for centred $\by$.
\item \emph{Linear regressor on features $\bphi$.}
    $\Hcal_d = \{(\langle \bphi(v), \bm{\beta}\rangle)_{v \in V} : \bm{\beta} \in \R^d\}$;
    $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)$ is the projection-$R^2$ of $\by$ on
    $\mathrm{col}(\bphi)$.
\item \emph{Kernel ridge with kernel $\mathcal{K}$.}  $\Hcal_d$ is the
    column span of the kernel matrix.
\item \emph{Sufficiently expressive multi-layer perceptron (MLP) or
    gradient-boosted machine (GBM).}  $\Hcal_d = \R^N$, so
    $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by) = 1$ and the bound becomes vacuous --- as
    expected, since an arbitrarily expressive learner has no structural
    floor.
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Where the bound is informative vs trivial]
\label{rem:trivial-vs-informative}
The bound is most informative in the regime $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by) \in
(0, 1)$, where the learner's reachable subspace is a non-trivial
proper subset of $\R^N$.  This is the operationally standard regime:
classical machine-learning models (linear / ridge / kernel-ridge / MLP
with bounded weights / gradient-boosted trees of bounded depth) all
operate in $\Hcal_d \subsetneq \R^N$ by construction.  Even
nominally over-parametrised neural networks have, in practice, an
\emph{implicit} $\Hcal_d$ much smaller than their parametric capacity
suggests (e.g., the subspace reachable by stochastic gradient descent
(SGD); \citealp{NeyshaburTomiokaSrebro2014}).  The bound captures
the structural floor in this empirically-active subspace, not in
the nominal worst case.  Conversely, an arbitrarily expressive learner
with $\Hcal_d = \R^N$ is not constrained by the bound (it returns the
trivial $\E[L_{T^c}] \geq 0$); but such a learner has no useful
generalisation in any leave-node-out regime regardless of our
framework, since it can fit arbitrary functions on $T$ with zero
residual signal for $T^c$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Assumptions}

\begin{assumption}[Bounded centred target]
\label{a:target}
$\|\by\|_\infty \leq M$ and $\bone^\top \by = 0$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[ERM learner]
\label{a:erm}
The learner $\Acal$ is an empirical risk minimiser on $\Hcal_d$:
$\hat f_\Acal(T) \in \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d} L_T(f)$.  The hypothesis
class is truncated to $[-M, M]$ and admits a Rademacher complexity
$\mathcal{R}_n(\Hcal_d)$ in the sense of \citet[][\S 3.3]{Mohri2018book}.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Proportional regime]
\label{a:proportional}
The protocol $\Pi$ satisfies $n/N = \alpha$ for some $\alpha \in
(0, 1)$ bounded away from $0$ and $1$, i.e., there exist
$\alpha_{\min}, \alpha_{\max} \in (0, 1)$ with $\alpha_{\min} \le
\alpha \le \alpha_{\max}$. All rate statements in
Theorems~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}--\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}
and Corollary~\ref{cor:minimax-rate-optimality} are understood
asymptotically as $N, n \to \infty$ with $\alpha = n/N$ fixed.
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}[Operational scope of A\ref{a:proportional}]
\label{rem:proportional-scope}
A\ref{a:proportional} covers all standard cross-validation
protocols: $k$-fold CV ($\alpha = 1 - 1/k$ ranging over
$\{0.5, 0.66, 0.8, 0.9, 0.95\}$ for $k \in \{2, 3, 5, 10, 20\}$),
random 80/20 holdout splits ($\alpha = 0.8$), and group-wise
GroupKFold protocols (matching the empirical instantiations of
\S\ref{sec:empirics}). The few-shot regime $n = o(N)$ is
\emph{outside} the scope of
Theorems~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}--\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}
as stated; the slack rate degrades there to $O(M^2/\sqrt{n})$ and
the minimax matching breaks (Section~\ref{subsec:open}).
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} (universal lower bound)
does \emph{not} require A\ref{a:proportional} and holds verbatim
for any exchangeable protocol regardless of $\alpha$.
\end{remark}

\section{Main results}
\label{sec:main-results}

\begin{theorem}[Universal spectral lower bound]
\label{thm:universal-lower-bound}
Under Assumptions~\ref{a:target}--\ref{a:erm}, for any evaluation
protocol $\Pi$ satisfying $\E_T[L_T(f)] = L_V(f)$ for all
$T$-independent $f \in \R^N$ (in particular $\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$),
\begin{equation}
    \E_{T \sim \Dcal_\Pi}\!\big[L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))\big]
    \;\geq\; \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)\big) \cdot \Var(\by).
    \label{eq:universal-lower-bound}
\end{equation}
The bound is \emph{exact in expectation}: there is no concentration
slack term.  The protocol hypothesis $\E_T[L_T(f)] = L_V(f)$ is
precisely \emph{spatial exchangeability} of $\Pi$: the marginal
inclusion probability $\PR_T[v \in T]$ is constant across $v \in V$,
necessarily equal to $n/N$.  It is satisfied by uniform without-%
replacement sampling ($\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$), $k$-fold cross-%
validation, and GroupKFold protocols, and fails for non-uniform
importance-weighted protocols (cf.\ Section~\ref{subsec:c3}).
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Universal upper bound, sharp transductive form]
\label{thm:universal-upper-bound}
Under Assumptions~\ref{a:target}--\ref{a:erm}, with probability $\geq 1
- \eta$ over $T \sim \Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$,
\begin{equation}
    L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))
    \;\leq\; \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)\big) \cdot \Var(\by)
        \;+\; 2 \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\ell \circ \Hcal_d)
        \;+\; 23.1\, M^2 \sqrt{\frac{(n + u)\log(2/\eta)}{n \cdot u}},
    \label{eq:universal-upper-bound}
\end{equation}
where $u := N - n$ is the held-out cardinality,
$\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\Hcal_d)$ is the transductive
Rademacher complexity of \citet[][\S 3]{ElYanivPechyony2006},
and $\ell$ is the truncated square loss, bounded in $[0, 4M^2]$.
The constant $23.1$ aggregates two contributions traced in the
proof of Section~\ref{sec:upper-bound-proof}: (i) the
$20.2 = 5.05 \cdot 4$ from the transductive Rademacher bound of
\citet[][Theorem~1]{ElYanivPechyony2006} (rescaled from the
unit-range hypothesis of their result to the loss range $[0, 4M^2]$
via the loss-range factor $4M^2/M^2 = 4$), and (ii) an additional
$2.83 = 4/\sqrt{2}$ factor that strictly upper-bounds and absorbs
the $O(M^2/\sqrt{n})$ without-replacement Serfling slack of the
population-risk minimiser, using
$20.2 + 2.83\sqrt{u/(n+u)} \leq 23.03 \leq 23.1$ since
$\sqrt{u/(n+u)} \leq 1$. For learners with
$\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\Hcal_d) = O(M\sqrt{d/u})$
(linear, kernel-ridge, MLP with bounded weights, gradient-boosted
trees of bounded depth), Talagrand contraction with Lipschitz
constant $4M$ yields $\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\ell \circ
\Hcal_d) = O(M^2\sqrt{d/u})$. The full slack is then
\[
    O\!\left(M^2\,\bigg(\sqrt{\tfrac{d}{u}} + \tfrac{1}{\sqrt n}
        + \tfrac{1}{\sqrt u}\bigg)\right),
\]
where the latter two terms come from
$\sqrt{(n+u)/(nu)} = \sqrt{1/n + 1/u} \le 1/\sqrt n + 1/\sqrt u$.
Under A\ref{a:proportional} (i.e., $n = \alpha N$ with $\alpha$
fixed away from $0$ and $1$), this simplifies to
$O(M^2/\sqrt{N - n}) = O(M^2/\sqrt N)$. In the few-shot regime
$n = o(N)$, the dominant slack term is $M^2/\sqrt n$, not
$M^2/\sqrt{N - n}$; the simplification fails and the rate
statement of Corollary~\ref{cor:minimax-rate-optimality} does
not apply.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Berry--Esseen minimax lower bound on the slack]
\label{thm:minimax-lower-bound}
Under A\ref{a:incoherence} (with dispersion parameter $\kappa > 0$),
A\ref{a:proportional} ($\alpha = n/N$ fixed), and
A\ref{a:capacity} (with leverage-incoherence parameter $\mu \ge 1$
and dimension $d = \dim \Hcal_d$ satisfying
$d \le D_0(\alpha, \kappa, \mu)\sqrt{N-n}$ for an explicit
$D_0$ given by Lemma~\ref{lem:leakage} below), and provided
$N - n \ge N_0(\kappa) := 100\, C_{\mathrm{BE}}^2/\kappa$ for the
without-replacement Berry--Esseen constant $C_{\mathrm{BE}} \le 14$,
for any $M > 0$, there exists a signal
$\by^\star \in \Hcal_d^\perp$ with $\|\by^\star\|_\infty = M$ such
that, for every estimator $\hat f \in \Hcal_d$ \emph{(including
one with oracle access to $\by^\star$ on $V$)},
\begin{equation}
    \PR_T\!\Big[
        L_{T^c}(\hat f) - \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by^\star)\big)\Var(\by^\star)
        \;\geq\; c(\alpha, \kappa) \cdot \frac{M^2}{\sqrt{N - n}}
    \Big]
    \;\geq\; c',
    \label{eq:minimax-lower-bound}
\end{equation}
where
\[
    c(\alpha, \kappa) \;:=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\tau\,\sqrt{\alpha\,\kappa},
    \qquad
    \tau \;:=\; \Phi^{-1}(3/4) \;\approx\; 0.674,
\]
is bounded away from $0$ on the operational range
$\alpha \in [\alpha_{\min}, \alpha_{\max}]$ of A\ref{a:proportional},
and $c' \ge 1/10$ is an absolute constant bounded away from $0$.
Consequently, no estimator --- including one with oracle access to
$\by^\star$ on $V$ --- can drive the held-out error below the
Berry--Esseen floor $\Omega(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$ \emph{with probability
bounded away from zero}, provided the hypothesis-class capacity is
bounded as in A\ref{a:capacity}.  This is a one-sided anti-%
concentration statement at constant probability: combined with the
high-probability upper bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound},
it establishes rate-optimality of the slack at $\Theta(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$,
in the sense of Corollary~\ref{cor:minimax-rate-optimality}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Minimax rate-optimality for graph-supervised learning]
\label{cor:minimax-rate-optimality}
Under A\ref{a:target}--A\ref{a:proportional},
A\ref{a:incoherence}, and A\ref{a:capacity}, combining
Theorems~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}--\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}:
the empirical-risk-minimising learner achieves the structural floor
of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} up to a
\emph{high-probability slack} of $O(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$
(Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}), while no estimator can
avoid a slack of $\Omega(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$ \emph{with at least
constant probability $c' \ge 1/10$} (Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}).
As $N, n \to \infty$ with $n/N \to \alpha \in (0, 1)$ fixed, the
slack rate at protocol $\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ on signal class
$\Ycal_{\Hcal_d, M} := \{\by \in \R^N : \|\by\|_\infty \le M,
\bone^\top \by = 0\}$ is therefore
\begin{equation}
    \frR_n(\Hcal_d, M)
    \;=\; \Theta\!\left(\frac{M^2}{\sqrt{N - n}}
        + \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\Hcal_d)\right),
    \label{eq:cramer-rao-rate}
\end{equation}
in the sense that the upper bound holds with high probability over
$T$ and the lower bound holds with at least constant probability
$c' \ge 1/10$ over $T$ uniformly in $\hat f$.  The empirical-risk-%
minimising learner is therefore \emph{minimax-rate-optimal} on
graph-supervised regression problems under leave-node-out
evaluation in the operational regime fixed by
A\ref{a:proportional}--A\ref{a:capacity}.

The multiplicative constant gap between upper and lower bounds (at
the matched rate) is
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{gap}(\alpha, \kappa)
    \;=\; \frac{23.1}{\tfrac{1}{2}\tau\sqrt{\alpha \kappa}}
    \;=\; \frac{46.2}{\tau\sqrt{\alpha \kappa}}
    \;\approx\; \frac{68.5}{\sqrt{\alpha \kappa}},
    \label{eq:cramer-rao-gap}
\end{equation}
with $\tau = \Phi^{-1}(3/4) \approx 0.674$.  This gap is
$\alpha$-dependent: at the operational midpoint $\alpha = 0.5$
with $\kappa \approx 0.25$ (binary $\pm 1$ signals supported on
half the nodes), it evaluates to approximately $194$; at the 80/20
split $\alpha = 0.8$ it tightens to $\approx 153$; in the few-fold
$\alpha = 0.95$ regime it falls to $\approx 140$.  The upper-side
constant $23.1 = 20.2 + 2.83$ aggregates the
\citet{ElYanivPechyony2006} Rademacher constant ($20.2$) and the
Serfling slack ($2.83$); each tightens to
$\sqrt{2} \times 4 + \sqrt 2 \approx 7.07$ via the
\citet{BardenetMaillard2015} without-replacement Bernstein
refinement (see \S\ref{subsec:open}~item~1), pulling the midpoint
gap to $\approx 59$.  The gap is bounded but not universal: it
scales as $1/\sqrt{\alpha\kappa}$, so it tightens at high $\alpha$
and on classes with large dispersion parameter $\kappa$, and
degrades as $\kappa \to 0$ or $\alpha \to 0$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[On the use of ``Cram\'er--Rao analog'' in the introduction]
\label{rem:cramer-rao-framing}
We use the phrase ``Cram\'er--Rao analog'' in the abstract and
introduction (Contribution 4, \S\ref{subsec:related-work}) to
signal the structural parallel with parametric efficiency
theory: a target-dependent floor
(Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}) is matched by an
explicit estimator (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}) at
the rate $M^2/\sqrt{N-n}$, and no estimator can drive the slack
below the same rate with probability bounded away from zero
(Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}).  The precise quantitative
statement is the rate-optimality of
Corollary~\ref{cor:minimax-rate-optimality} for fixed
$\alpha = n/N$, not an exact-constant efficiency statement: the
multiplicative constant gap $\mathrm{gap}(\alpha, \kappa)
\propto 1/\sqrt{\alpha\kappa}$ depends on the train/test split
ratio and the dispersion parameter; at the operational midpoint
$\alpha = 0.5$ it evaluates to $\approx 194$ (resp.\ $\approx 59$
after the Bardenet--Maillard tightening), meaning the analogy is
\emph{structural}, not numerical.  A further subtlety distinguishes
the two sides: the upper bound is a \emph{high-probability}
statement on the slack of the ERM, while the lower bound is a
\emph{constant-probability} anti-concentration statement on the
held-out residual fluctuation (Remark~\ref{rem:constant-probability}).
This asymmetry is intrinsic to the problem: the slack of a
centred random fluctuation cannot have a positive one-sided
lower bound at high probability, only at constant probability.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Centring]
\label{rem:centring}
If $\by$ is not centred, replace $\by$ throughout by $\by - \bar y \bone$,
where $\bar y := N^{-1}\bone^\top \by$.  Equivalently, augment
$\Hcal_d$ to contain the constant vector $\bone$, which absorbs the
mean predictor.  All standard learners satisfy this implicitly through
a bias / intercept term.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Why the Pesenson sampling quality $\sigma_T$ does not appear]
\label{rem:no-sigma-T-needed}
An earlier formulation of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}
(working notes \texttt{notes/01\_universal\_bound\_proof.tex} in the
companion repository, withdrawn in \texttt{notes/03\_non\_realisable\_resolution.tex})
expressed the slack as $O(\rho(\Pi) /
\sqrt{n \cdot \sigma_\Pi(\Hcal_d)})$, where $\sigma_\Pi(\Hcal_d) :=
\E_T[\lambda_{\min}(\bB^\top \bP_T \bB)]$ is the
Pesenson--Anis--Gadde--Ortega sampling quality of $\Pi$ for $\Hcal_d$.
This formulation was an artefact of using the Tikhonov-regularised
Pesenson-ridge estimator as the witness predictor.  In fact, the
population-risk minimiser $f^\star_{\Hcal_d}$ itself serves as a
witness without invoking the sampling-restricted inverse $(\bB^\top
\bP_T \bB)^{-1}$, eliminating the $\sigma_T$ factor entirely.  The
proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} below uses this
cleaner witness, mirroring the proof of Theorem~1(c) of
\citet{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound}.  The
graph-Fisher-information / inverse-problem theory of
\citet{BauerPereverzev2005,Cavalier2008} --- relevant for noisy
continuous Hilbert-space inverse problems --- does not apply to the
finite-population empirical-risk problem treated here.
\end{remark}

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}}
\label{sec:lower-bound-proof}

The proof has three short steps.

\paragraph{Step 1 — Functional floor on $L_V$ (Bessel-projection inequality).}

\begin{lemma}[Bessel projection floor]
\label{lem:bessel}
For any $\hat f \in \Hcal_d$,
$N \cdot L_V(\hat f) = \|\hat f - \by\|^2 \;\geq\; \|f^\star_{\Hcal_d} - \by\|^2 = N \cdot (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The population-risk minimiser $f^\star_{\Hcal_d} := \arg\min_{f \in
\Hcal_d} L_V(f)$ exists by the extreme value theorem: $\Hcal_d$ is
closed under truncation to $[-M, M]$ (Definition~\ref{def:learner})
and therefore embeds as a closed bounded subset of $[-M, M]^N$,
which is compact in $\R^N$; the loss $L_V$ is continuous as a
quadratic polynomial in the prediction vector; hence the infimum is
attained.  By definition of the argmin, $L_V(\hat f) \geq
L_V(f^\star_{\Hcal_d}) = (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)$ for
every $\hat f \in \Hcal_d$.  By Assumption~\ref{a:erm},
$\hat f_\Acal(T) \in \Hcal_d$, so the inequality applies pointwise
in $T$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Step 2 — Transduction identity.}

\begin{lemma}[Population--train--holdout decomposition]
\label{lem:transduction}
For any $f \in \R^N$ and any $T \subset V$ with $|T| = n$,
\begin{equation}
    L_V(f) \;=\; \frac{n}{N}\, L_T(f) \;+\; \frac{N-n}{N}\, L_{T^c}(f),
    \label{eq:transduction-identity}
\end{equation}
equivalently $L_{T^c}(f) = \rho(\Pi) L_V(f) - (\rho(\Pi) - 1) L_T(f)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$N \cdot L_V(f) = \sum_{v \in V}(f(v) - y_v)^2 = \sum_{v \in T}(\ldots)
+ \sum_{v \in T^c}(\ldots) = n \cdot L_T(f) + (N-n) \cdot L_{T^c}(f)$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Step 3 — Combination.}

By Lemma~\ref{lem:transduction} applied to $f = \hat f_\Acal(T)$ and
taken in expectation over $T$,
\begin{equation}
    \E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))]
    \;=\; \rho(\Pi) \cdot \E_T[L_V(\hat f_\Acal(T))]
        \;-\; (\rho(\Pi) - 1) \cdot \E_T[L_T(\hat f_\Acal(T))].
    \label{eq:lower-step3-expectation}
\end{equation}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:bessel}, $L_V(\hat f_\Acal(T)) \geq (1 -
\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)$ pointwise in $T$, hence
$\E_T[L_V(\hat f_\Acal(T))] \geq (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)$.

For the $L_T$ term, by Assumption~\ref{a:erm} (ERM property) and
$f^\star_{\Hcal_d} \in \Hcal_d$,
$L_T(\hat f_\Acal(T)) \leq L_T(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})$ pointwise in $T$.
Taking expectation and using the protocol hypothesis $\E_T[L_T(f)]
= L_V(f)$ applied to the $T$-independent predictor $f^\star_{\Hcal_d}$
(which is well-defined globally on $V$, not as a function of $T$),
\begin{equation}
    \E_T[L_T(\hat f_\Acal(T))]
    \;\leq\; \E_T[L_T(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})]
    \;=\; L_V(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})
    \;=\; (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)) \Var(\by).
    \label{eq:lower-step3-LT}
\end{equation}
Setting $A := (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)) \Var(\by)$ and substituting
into \eqref{eq:lower-step3-expectation},
$$
\E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))] \;\geq\; \rho(\Pi) \cdot A - (\rho(\Pi) - 1) \cdot A \;=\; A.
$$
This is Eq.~\eqref{eq:universal-lower-bound}.\qed

\begin{remark}[Why the bound is exact in expectation]
\label{rem:exact-in-expectation}
The two slack terms (from upper-bounding $L_V$ above by the Bessel
floor, and from upper-bounding $L_T$ below by the ERM-on-projection
inequality) cancel exactly through the transduction identity.  This
mirrors the structural unbiasedness condition under which the classical
Cram\'er--Rao bound holds with equality.  A high-probability version
of the bound carries a $O(\rho M^2/\sqrt{n})$ slack from the
Hoeffding--Serfling concentration of \eqref{eq:lower-step3-LT}; see
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} for the matching upper bound.
\end{remark}

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}}
\label{sec:upper-bound-proof}

The proof structure mirrors that of part~(c) of Theorem~1 of
\citet[][\S 5.2]{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound}, with the
population-risk minimiser $f^\star_{\Hcal_d}$ replacing the specific
compositional projector $\bP_{\Scal_{\mathrm{comp}}}\by$ used there.

\paragraph{Step 1 — In-sample concentration of the witness.}

The population-risk minimiser $f^\star_{\Hcal_d} \in \Hcal_d$ is fixed
(not a function of $T$).  By Serfling's without-replacement Hoeffding
inequality \citep{Serfling1974,BardenetMaillard2015} applied to the
bounded sequence $\{(f^\star_{\Hcal_d}(v) - y_v)^2\}_{v \in V}$ ---
which has population mean $L_V(f^\star_{\Hcal_d}) = (1 -
\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)$ and supremum at most $4 M^2$ --- with
probability $\geq 1 - \eta/2$ over $T \sim \Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$,
\begin{equation}
    L_T(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})
    \;\leq\; (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)) \Var(\by)
        + 4 M^2 \sqrt{\frac{(1 - (n-1)/N) \log(2/\eta)}{2 n}}.
    \label{eq:upper-step1-serfling}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 2 — Transductive Rademacher generalisation on $\hat f_\Acal(T)$.}

The transductive Rademacher bound of
\citet[][Theorem~1]{ElYanivPechyony2006}, combined with the
Talagrand contraction lemma \citep{Talagrand1996concentration}
(the truncated square loss $\ell : (z, y) \mapsto (z - y)^2$ is
$4M$-Lipschitz in $z$ on $[-M, M]$ when $y \in [-M, M]$, since
$|\partial_z (z-y)^2| = |2(z-y)| \le 4M$), yields with probability
$\geq 1 - \eta/2$ over $T \sim \Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$:
\begin{equation}
    L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))
    \;\leq\; L_T(\hat f_\Acal(T))
        + 2 \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\ell \circ \Hcal_d)
        + 20.2\, M^2 \sqrt{\frac{(n + u) \log(2/\eta)}{n u}}.
    \label{eq:upper-step2-trans-rademacher}
\end{equation}
The Rademacher constant $20.2 = 5.05 \cdot 4$ is the unit-range
El-Yaniv--Pechyony constant $5.05$ multiplied by the loss-range
factor $4M^2/M^2 = 4$ (the loss $\ell$ takes values in $[0, 4M^2]$
under $|f|, |y| \le M$; rescaling to $[0, 1]$ via $\ell/(4M^2)$ to
apply EYP's unit-range result and rescaling back yields the factor
$4$).  Crucially, this bound \emph{directly} controls
$L_{T^c} - L_T$, avoiding the $\sqrt{\rho(\Pi) - 1}$ slack inflation
incurred by the inductive Mohri bound
\citep[][Theorem~3.7]{Mohri2018book} which would route through the
population risk $L_V$. Under $\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$, the uniform
inclusion probability $\pi(v) = n/N$ makes the importance-weighted
training loss $L_T^\pi(f) := N^{-1}\sum_{v \in T}(f(v)-y_v)^2/\pi(v)$
coincide with the unweighted $L_T(f)$, so no $\pi$-reweighting is
needed in either~\eqref{eq:upper-step2-trans-rademacher} or
Step~3 below.

\paragraph{Step 3 — ERM property and conclusion.}

By Assumption~\ref{a:erm} the ERM property gives
$L_T(\hat f_\Acal(T)) \leq L_T(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})$ pointwise in $T$,
since $f^\star_{\Hcal_d} \in \Hcal_d$. Combining this with the
Serfling bound \eqref{eq:upper-step1-serfling} of Step~1 (which
controls $L_T(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})$ in terms of the population value
$L_V(f^\star_{\Hcal_d}) = (1-\Rspec(\Hcal_d,\by))\Var(\by)$) and
the Rademacher bound \eqref{eq:upper-step2-trans-rademacher} of
Step~2, on the intersection of both high-probability events
(union bound: total probability $\ge 1 - \eta$),
\begin{align*}
    L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))
    &\stackrel{\text{Step 2}}{\leq}
        L_T(\hat f_\Acal(T)) + 2\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\ell\circ\Hcal_d)
        + 20.2\, M^2 \sqrt{\tfrac{(n+u)\log(2/\eta)}{nu}}\\
    &\stackrel{\text{ERM}}{\leq}\;
        L_T(f^\star_{\Hcal_d}) + 2\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\ell\circ\Hcal_d)
        + 20.2\, M^2 \sqrt{\tfrac{(n+u)\log(2/\eta)}{nu}}\\
    &\stackrel{\text{Step 1}}{\leq}\;
        (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)
        + 2\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\ell\circ\Hcal_d)\\
    &\qquad+\; 20.2\, M^2 \sqrt{\tfrac{(n+u)\log(2/\eta)}{nu}}
        \;+\; 4 M^2 \sqrt{\tfrac{(1 - (n-1)/N)\log(2/\eta)}{2n}}.
\end{align*}
It remains to merge the Serfling and Rademacher slack terms into a
single expression of the same functional form. Using
$(1-(n-1)/N) \le 1$ and $4/\sqrt{2} = 2\sqrt{2} \approx 2.83$,
\[
    4 M^2 \sqrt{\frac{(1-(n-1)/N)\log(2/\eta)}{2n}}
    \;\le\; 2.83\, M^2 \sqrt{\frac{\log(2/\eta)}{n}}
    \;=\; 2.83\,\sqrt{\frac{u}{n+u}}\, M^2 \sqrt{\frac{(n+u)\log(2/\eta)}{nu}},
\]
where the last step factors out $\sqrt{(n+u)\log(2/\eta)/(nu)}$.
Adding the Rademacher term and using $\sqrt{u/(n+u)} \le 1$,
\[
    \Big[20.2 + 2.83\sqrt{u/(n+u)}\Big]\, M^2 \sqrt{\tfrac{(n+u)\log(2/\eta)}{nu}}
    \;\le\;\big(20.2 + 2.83\big)\, M^2 \sqrt{\tfrac{(n+u)\log(2/\eta)}{nu}}
    \;\le\; 23.1\, M^2 \sqrt{\tfrac{(n+u)\log(2/\eta)}{nu}},
\]
yielding the aggregated slack constant $23.1$ in
\eqref{eq:universal-upper-bound}.  Figure~\ref{fig:slack-comparison}
plots the slack rate of (a) the original Mohri-routed bound, (b) the
sharp transductive form proved here, and (c) the Berry--Esseen minimax
lower bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}, illustrating the
factor-$\sqrt{\rho-1}$ elimination by the transductive Rademacher
upgrade.  See
\texttt{notes/04\_sharp\_transductive\_constant.tex} for the
derivation of the factor-$\sqrt{\rho-1}$ improvement over an
intermediate version of this theorem that routed through inductive
Mohri.\qed

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}}
\label{sec:minimax-proof}

We construct an adversarial signal whose Berry--Esseen anticoncentration
on the held-out residual already forces an $\Omega(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$
deviation between $L_{T^c}(\hat f)$ and its population value,
irrespective of estimator choice.

\paragraph{Dispersion assumption on $\Hcal_d^\perp$.}

For the Berry--Esseen anticoncentration below to deliver a
non-vacuous lower bound, the adversarial signal $\by^\star =
M\bu$ must induce a strictly positive fluctuation
$X_v := (y^\star_v)^2 - \Var(\by^\star)$ across $v \in V$. This is
equivalent to requiring that the empirical fourth-power coefficients
$\{|u_v|^2\}_{v \in V}$ have non-trivial variance: a vector $\bu$
with constant magnitude $|u_v|^2 \equiv \mathrm{const}$ would make
$X_v \equiv 0$ and the anticoncentration would collapse.

\begin{assumption}[Dispersion of $\Hcal_d^\perp$]
\label{a:incoherence}
There exists $\bu \in \Hcal_d^\perp$ with $\bone^\top \bu = 0$,
$\|\bu\|_\infty \le 1$, $\|\bu\|_2^2 \ge \gamma N$, and
\begin{equation}
    \frac{1}{N}\sum_{v \in V}|u_v|^4
    \;-\; \Big(\frac{\|\bu\|_2^2}{N}\Big)^{\!2}
    \;\ge\; \kappa,
    \label{eq:incoherence-dispersion}
\end{equation}
for absolute constants $\gamma \in (0, 1]$ and $\kappa > 0$. The
left-hand side is the variance of $\{|u_v|^2\}_{v \in V}$ across
$V$, i.e., the dispersion of the squared magnitudes.
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}[When A\ref{a:incoherence} holds]
\label{rem:incoherence-when}
A\ref{a:incoherence} excludes two degenerate regimes: (i) when
every non-zero vector in $\Hcal_d^\perp$ has constant magnitude
$|u_v|^2 = \mathrm{const}$ across $v$ (e.g.,
$\Hcal_d^\perp = \mathrm{span}(\bu)$ with $\bu$ a balanced
$\pm c$ vector), in which case
\eqref{eq:incoherence-dispersion} fails with $\kappa = 0$ and
Berry--Esseen anticoncentration genuinely vanishes; (ii) when
every vector in $\Hcal_d^\perp$ concentrates on $o(N)$
coordinates, in which case $\gamma = o(1)$ and the bound
degrades as $\gamma \to 0$. The assumption is satisfied for
all operationally standard classes:
\begin{itemize}[label=$\bullet$,leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Generic random subspaces:} for $\Hcal_d$ spanned by
    $d$ vectors with i.i.d.\ continuous entries, the
    fourth-central-moment of $\bu \in \Hcal_d^\perp$ satisfies
    \eqref{eq:incoherence-dispersion} with $\kappa \asymp
    2(\E[U^2])^2$ asymptotically by standard chi-square moment
    computations.
\item \emph{Bandlimited subspaces on expander graphs:} the
    leverage-score concentration of \citet{Cohen2016sparsification}
    guarantees $\bu \in \Hcal_d^\perp$ with both $\|\bu\|_\infty
    \le 1$ and non-trivial $|u_v|^2$ dispersion.
\item \emph{Categorical-only $\Hcal_d \subseteq \mathrm{span}(\bone)$:}
    any centred $\bu$ with three distinct magnitudes (e.g.\
    a balanced $\{0, \pm 1\}$ pattern with non-trivial support
    fraction $\gamma < 1$) satisfies
    \eqref{eq:incoherence-dispersion} with $\kappa = \gamma(1-\gamma)$.
\end{itemize}
Conversely, it fails for $\Hcal_d^\perp = \mathrm{span}(\bu^\circ)$
where $\bu^\circ$ has constant non-zero magnitude across all of $V$
(the pathological case identified in the proof construction
below).
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Capacity and leverage-incoherence assumption.}

The dispersion condition A\ref{a:incoherence} constrains the
\emph{adversarial} side of the construction: it guarantees that the
residual energy $\{(y^\star_v)^2\}_{v \in V}$ fluctuates non-trivially
across $V$, so that the Berry--Esseen anticoncentration delivers a
strictly positive lower bound on $\Delta_T = L_{T^c}(0) -
\Var(\by^\star)$.  A symmetric assumption is needed on the
\emph{learner} side, controlling the capacity of $\Hcal_d$ to absorb
the residual on $T^c$ via the so-called \emph{oracle leakage}
$\|\Pi_{\Hcal_d|_{T^c}}(\by^\star|_{T^c})\|^2$.  Without such control,
a $V$-aware oracle could exploit the discrepancy between global and
local orthogonality (since $\by^\star \in \Hcal_d^\perp$ in
$\ell_2(V)$ does \emph{not} imply $\by^\star|_{T^c} \in
(\Hcal_d|_{T^c})^\perp$) to drive $L_{T^c}(\hat f)$ below the
Berry--Esseen floor.

\begin{assumption}[Capacity and leverage incoherence of $\Hcal_d$]
\label{a:capacity}
Let $\bB \in \R^{N \times d}$ be an orthonormal basis of $\Hcal_d$
(i.e.\ $\bB^\top \bB = I_d$), and let $\ell_v := \|\bB^\top e_v\|_2^2$
denote the \emph{leverage scores} of $\Hcal_d$ (so $\sum_v \ell_v = d$).
Assume the dimension and the leverage-incoherence bound
\begin{equation}
    d \;=\; \dim \Hcal_d \;\le\; D_0(\alpha, \kappa, \eta, \mu)\sqrt{N - n},
    \qquad
    \max_{v \in V}\, \ell_v \;\le\; \mu\,\frac{d}{N},
    \label{eq:capacity-leverage}
\end{equation}
for an absolute leverage-incoherence constant $\mu \ge 1$ and a
problem-dependent constant $D_0(\alpha, \kappa, \eta, \mu) > 0$ made
explicit in Lemma~\ref{lem:leakage} below.
\end{assumption}

\begin{remark}[Operational scope of A\ref{a:capacity}]
\label{rem:capacity-when}
The bound $d = O(\sqrt{N-n})$ is the regime in which the structural
floor of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} is informative and
the held-out evaluation is statistically non-trivial: for $d \gg
\sqrt{N-n}$, the slack of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}
itself becomes vacuous since $\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\Hcal_d)
\asymp M\sqrt{d/u}$.  The leverage-incoherence bound $\max_v \ell_v
\le \mu d/N$ is the standard incoherence condition in matrix
sketching, leverage-score sampling, and compressive sensing; it is
satisfied with $\mu = O(1)$ for: (i) Gaussian random projections of
$\R^N$ onto $d$-dimensional subspaces \citep{Tropp2015};
(ii) bandlimited subspaces on expander graphs
\citep{Cohen2016sparsification}; (iii) generic Fourier-band
restrictions on bounded-degree similarity graphs.  It fails only for
\emph{coherent} classes that align with the standard basis (e.g.\
one-hot indicator predictors), where the leverage scores
concentrate on $O(d)$ coordinates and $\max_v \ell_v = 1$ regardless
of $d/N$.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Construction of the adversarial signal $\by^\star$.}

Under A\ref{a:incoherence}, pick $\bu \in \Hcal_d^\perp$ satisfying
\eqref{eq:incoherence-dispersion}: $\|\bu\|_\infty \le 1$,
$\bone^\top\bu = 0$, $\|\bu\|^2 \ge \gamma N$, and the
$|u_v|^2$-dispersion at least $\kappa$. Set $\by^\star := M\bu$.
Then $\by^\star \in \Hcal_d^\perp$, $\|\by^\star\|_\infty \le M$,
$\bone^\top\by^\star = 0$, and
\[
    \Var(\by^\star) \;=\; \|\by^\star\|^2/N \;=\; M^2 \|\bu\|^2/N
        \;\ge\; M^2 \gamma.
\]
Since $\by^\star \in \Hcal_d^\perp$, $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by^\star) = 0$
and the lower-bound reference value is $\Var(\by^\star) \ge \gamma M^2$.

\paragraph{Population floor and the held-out fluctuation $\Delta_T$.}

The zero predictor $0 \in \Hcal_d$ achieves population loss
$L_V(0) = N^{-1}\sum_v (y^\star_v)^2 = \Var(\by^\star)$, and the
Bessel projection floor of Lemma~\ref{lem:bessel} certifies that no
in-class predictor improves on this in population: $\inf_{f \in
\Hcal_d} L_V(f) = L_V(0) = \Var(\by^\star)$ (this fails locally on
$T^c$ alone, see the leakage analysis below). Berry--Esseen anti-%
concentration will be applied to the held-out fluctuation of the
zero predictor,
\[
    \Delta_T \;:=\; L_{T^c}(0) - \Var(\by^\star)
    \;=\; \frac{1}{|T^c|}\sum_{v \in T^c}(y^\star_v)^2
        \;-\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{v \in V}(y^\star_v)^2.
\]
Define the centred random variables $X_v := (y^\star_v)^2 -
\Var(\by^\star) = M^2 |u_v|^2 - M^2 \|\bu\|^2/N$. Under
A\ref{a:incoherence}, $\|\bu\|_\infty \le 1$ yields $|X_v| \le
M^2$, and the population variance of $\{X_v\}_{v \in V}$ is
\begin{align}
    s^2 \;:=\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{v \in V} X_v^2
    \;=\; M^4 \,\bigg[
        \frac{1}{N}\sum_v |u_v|^4
        \;-\; \Big(\frac{\|\bu\|^2}{N}\Big)^{\!2}
    \bigg]
    \;\ge\; M^4 \kappa,
    \label{eq:minimax-popvar}
\end{align}
where the lower bound is the dispersion guarantee
\eqref{eq:incoherence-dispersion} of A\ref{a:incoherence}. The
variance is therefore strictly positive, with absolute lower bound
$s \ge M^2 \sqrt{\kappa}$.

\paragraph{Berry--Esseen on sampling without replacement.}

By the Berry--Esseen theorem for the standardised sum of a uniform
sample without replacement from a finite population
\citep{Bikelis1969,Bentkus2003}, with $\sigma^2_T := s^2 \cdot n /
(N (N-n))$ the without-replacement variance of
$|T^c|^{-1}\sum_{T^c} X_v$,
\[
    \sup_{x \in \R}\,\bigg|
        \PR_T\!\Big[\sigma_T^{-1}\, \Delta_T \le x\Big]
        \,-\, \Phi(x)
    \bigg|
    \;\le\; C_{\mathrm{BE}}\,\frac{M^2}{s\sqrt{N-n}}
    \;\le\; \frac{C_{\mathrm{BE}}}{\sqrt{\kappa}\sqrt{N-n}}
\]
for an absolute constant $C_{\mathrm{BE}} \le 14$
\citep[][Cor.~1.2]{Bentkus2003}.

\paragraph{Anticoncentration of $\Delta_T$ at constant probability.}

Since $\Delta_T$ is centred (cf.\ Step~``Population floor''), no
\emph{one-sided high-probability} lower bound of the form
$\PR[\Delta_T \ge t] \ge 1 - \eta$ can hold for any positive $t$:
the upper tail $\PR[\Delta_T \ge t]$ is at most $1/2 + O(\delta_{\mathrm{BE}})$
for $t > 0$.  Berry--Esseen anti-concentration instead yields a
\emph{constant-probability} statement, sufficient to certify the
$\Omega(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$ minimax rate.

Pick the fixed positive threshold $\tau := \Phi^{-1}(3/4) \approx
0.674$, chosen so that $1 - \Phi(\tau) = 1/4$.  By the Berry--Esseen
bound above,
\[
    \PR_T[\Delta_T \ge \tau\sigma_T]
    \;=\; 1 - \PR_T[\Delta_T < \tau\sigma_T]
    \;\ge\; 1 - \Phi(\tau) - \delta_{\mathrm{BE}}
    \;=\; \tfrac{1}{4} - \delta_{\mathrm{BE}},
\]
where $\delta_{\mathrm{BE}} := C_{\mathrm{BE}}/(\sqrt\kappa\sqrt{N-n})$.
Under the hypothesis $N - n \ge N_0(\kappa) := 100\,C_{\mathrm{BE}}^2/\kappa$
of Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}, the remainder satisfies
$\delta_{\mathrm{BE}} \le 1/10$, hence $\PR_T[\Delta_T \ge \tau\sigma_T]
\ge 1/4 - 1/10 = 3/20$.

Substituting $\sigma_T = s\sqrt{n/(N(N-n))} \ge M^2\sqrt{\kappa
n/(N(N-n))} = M^2\sqrt{\alpha\kappa}/\sqrt{N-n}$ (using $s \ge
M^2\sqrt{\kappa}$ from \eqref{eq:minimax-popvar} and $n/N = \alpha$)
yields, with $c_0(\alpha, \kappa) := \tau \sqrt{\alpha\kappa}$,
\begin{equation}
    \PR_T\!\Bigg[\Delta_T \;\ge\; c_0(\alpha, \kappa)
        \cdot \frac{M^2}{\sqrt{N - n}}\Bigg]
    \;\ge\; \tfrac{3}{20}.
    \label{eq:minimax-be-prob}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Oracle decomposition and the leakage term.}

For any estimator $\hat f \in \Hcal_d$ (including a $V$-aware
oracle that knows $\by^\star$ entirely), the held-out slack admits
the deterministic decomposition
\begin{align}
    L_{T^c}(\hat f) - \Var(\by^\star)
    &\;\ge\; \inf_{f \in \Hcal_d} L_{T^c}(f) \;-\; \Var(\by^\star)
    \notag\\
    &\;=\; \big[L_{T^c}(0) - \Var(\by^\star)\big]
        \;-\; \frac{1}{|T^c|}\,\big\|\Pi_{\Hcal_d|_{T^c}}\by^\star|_{T^c}\big\|_{T^c}^2
    \notag\\
    &\;=\; \Delta_T \;-\; \mathrm{leak}(T;\, \Hcal_d, \by^\star),
    \label{eq:oracle-decomposition}
\end{align}
where $\mathrm{leak}(T;\, \Hcal_d, \by^\star) :=
\|\Pi_{\Hcal_d|_{T^c}}\by^\star|_{T^c}\|_{T^c}^2/|T^c|$ is the
\emph{oracle leakage} on $T^c$: the loss reduction achievable by an
oracle that projects $\by^\star|_{T^c}$ onto the restriction class
$\Hcal_d|_{T^c}$.  Note that $\mathrm{leak} \ge 0$ in general,
even though $\by^\star \in \Hcal_d^\perp$ globally: restriction to
$T^c$ destroys orthogonality, and an oracle can recover a
non-trivial in-class projection on the held-out subset.

\begin{lemma}[Leakage bound under leverage incoherence]
\label{lem:leakage}
Under A\ref{a:incoherence}--A\ref{a:capacity} with leverage-%
incoherence constant $\mu \ge 1$, the expected oracle leakage of
$\by^\star = M\bu$ on $T^c \sim \Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
    \E_T\!\big[\mathrm{leak}(T;\, \Hcal_d, \by^\star)\big]
    \;\le\; \frac{2\,\mu\, M^2\, d}{(1-\alpha)\,(N-1)}\,\frac{n}{N}
    \;=\; O\!\Big(\frac{\mu\,M^2\,d\,\alpha}{(1-\alpha)\,N}\Big),
    \label{eq:leakage-bound}
\end{equation}
provided the matrix Chernoff regime $u = N - n \ge C_\mathrm{MC}\,
\mu\, d \log d$ holds for an absolute constant $C_\mathrm{MC} > 0$
\citep[Theorem~1.1]{Tropp2015}. Consequently, by Markov,
\begin{equation}
    \PR_T\!\Big[
        \mathrm{leak}(T;\, \Hcal_d, \by^\star)
        \;\ge\; \frac{c_0(\alpha,\kappa)}{2}\cdot \frac{M^2}{\sqrt{N-n}}
    \Big]
    \;\le\; \tfrac{1}{20},
    \label{eq:leakage-tail}
\end{equation}
whenever the capacity hypothesis A\ref{a:capacity} holds with
\begin{equation}
    D_0(\alpha, \kappa, \mu) \;:=\; \frac{\tau\,\sqrt{\kappa}}{80\,\mu\,\sqrt{\alpha}}
    \label{eq:D0-explicit}
\end{equation}
in \eqref{eq:capacity-leverage}, where $\tau = \Phi^{-1}(3/4)$
matches Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}.  Indeed, by Markov,
$\PR[\mathrm{leak} \ge (c_0/2) M^2/\sqrt{N-n}] \le \E[\mathrm{leak}]
\cdot 2\sqrt{N-n}/(c_0 M^2)$, and substituting
\eqref{eq:leakage-bound} together with
$d \le D_0\sqrt{N-n}$ yields the right-hand side $\le 1/20$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:leakage}]
Let $\bB \in \R^{N \times d}$ be the orthonormal basis of $\Hcal_d$
fixed in A\ref{a:capacity}, with leverage scores $\ell_v = \|\bB^\top
e_v\|^2$. Since $\by^\star = M\bu$ with $\bu \in \Hcal_d^\perp$, we
have $\bB^\top \by^\star = 0$, hence by additivity $\bB^\top\bP_T
\by^\star + \bB^\top \bP_{T^c}\by^\star = 0$, i.e.,
$\bB^\top\bP_{T^c}\by^\star = -\bB^\top\bP_T\by^\star$.

For uniform sampling-without-replacement of $T$ of size $n$ from
$V$, the without-replacement variance of the sum $\bB^\top\bP_T
\by^\star = \sum_{v \in T} \bb_v y^\star_v$ is
\begin{align*}
    \E_T\!\big[\|\bB^\top \bP_T \by^\star\|_2^2\big]
    &\;=\; \frac{n(N-n)}{N(N-1)}
        \bigg[\sum_v \ell_v (y^\star_v)^2 - \frac{1}{N}\Big(\sum_v \bb_v y^\star_v\Big)^{\!2}\bigg]\\
    &\;=\; \frac{n(N-n)}{N(N-1)}\sum_v \ell_v (y^\star_v)^2
        \;\le\; \frac{n(N-n)\, M^2\, d}{N(N-1)},
\end{align*}
using $(y^\star_v)^2 \le M^2$ and $\sum_v \ell_v = d$ in the last
step; the cross-correction $\sum_v \bb_v y^\star_v = \bB^\top
\by^\star$ vanishes by the orthogonality $\bu \in \Hcal_d^\perp$.

Next, by the matrix Chernoff inequality
\citep[][Theorem~1.1]{Tropp2015} applied to the random matrix
$\bB^\top \bP_{T^c}\bB = \sum_{v \in T^c} \bb_v\bb_v^\top$ with
$\|\bb_v\bb_v^\top\|_2 = \ell_v \le \mu d/N$ (using A\ref{a:capacity})
and expected sum $(u/N) I_d$, in the regime $u \ge C_\mathrm{MC}\,
\mu\, d \log d$,
\[
    \bB^\top \bP_{T^c}\bB \;\succeq\; \tfrac{1}{2}\, \tfrac{u}{N}\, I_d
    \qquad\text{w.p.}\;\ge\; 1 - 1/(2d).
\]
On this event,
\[
    \mathrm{leak}(T) \;=\;
    \frac{1}{|T^c|}\,
    (\bB^\top\bP_T\by^\star)^\top (\bB^\top\bP_{T^c}\bB)^{-1} (\bB^\top\bP_T\by^\star)
    \;\le\; \frac{2N}{u\,|T^c|}\,\|\bB^\top\bP_T\by^\star\|_2^2
    \;=\; \frac{2N}{u^2}\,\|\bB^\top\bP_T\by^\star\|_2^2,
\]
where we used $|T^c| = u$. Taking expectation, absorbing the
Chernoff failure event by truncation of the integrable bound
$\mathrm{leak}(T) \le N M^2$ (since
$\|\Pi_{\Hcal_d|_{T^c}}\by^\star\|^2 \le \|\by^\star\|^2 \le N M^2$),
\[
    \E_T\!\big[\mathrm{leak}(T)\big]
    \;\le\; \frac{2N}{u^2}\,\E_T\!\big[\|\bB^\top\bP_T\by^\star\|_2^2\big]
        \;+\; \frac{M^2 N}{2d}
    \;\le\; \frac{2N}{u^2}\,\frac{n(N-n)\,M^2\,d}{N(N-1)} + o(1)
    \;=\; \frac{2\,M^2\, d\, n}{u\,(N-1)},
\]
where the leading term dominates the truncation error in the
regime $u \ge C_\mathrm{MC}\,\mu\,d\log d$. The leverage-incoherence
constant $\mu$ enters through the Chernoff event probability, not
through the leading expectation; the cleaner statement
\eqref{eq:leakage-bound} absorbs $\mu$ into the constant. Markov's
inequality then yields \eqref{eq:leakage-tail} provided the
capacity constraint $d \le D_0\sqrt{N-n}$ holds with $D_0$ as
stated.\qed
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Intersection of events: defeating the oracle.}

Let $A := \{\Delta_T \ge c_0(\alpha,\kappa) M^2/\sqrt{N-n}\}$ and
$B := \{\mathrm{leak}(T) \le (c_0/2) M^2/\sqrt{N-n}\}$.  By
\eqref{eq:minimax-be-prob}, $\PR(A) \ge 3/20$.  By
\eqref{eq:leakage-tail}, $\PR(B^c) \le 1/20$, hence $\PR(B) \ge 19/20$.
By the Bonferroni inequality
$\PR(A \cap B) \ge \PR(A) + \PR(B) - 1 \ge 3/20 + 19/20 - 1 = 1/10$.
On the event $A \cap B$, the oracle decomposition
\eqref{eq:oracle-decomposition} delivers
\[
    L_{T^c}(\hat f) - \Var(\by^\star)
    \;\ge\; \Delta_T - \mathrm{leak}(T)
    \;\ge\; c_0\frac{M^2}{\sqrt{N-n}} \;-\; \tfrac{c_0}{2}\,\frac{M^2}{\sqrt{N-n}}
    \;=\; \frac{c_0(\alpha,\kappa)}{2}\,\frac{M^2}{\sqrt{N - n}},
\]
uniformly in $\hat f \in \Hcal_d$ (the dependence on $\hat f$
enters \emph{only} through the in-class infimum $\inf_{f \in \Hcal_d}
L_{T^c}(f)$, which is the oracle's loss).  Setting
$c(\alpha,\kappa) := c_0(\alpha,\kappa)/2 = \tfrac{1}{2}\tau\sqrt{\alpha\kappa}$
with $\tau = \Phi^{-1}(3/4)$, and $c' := 1/10$, recovers
\eqref{eq:minimax-lower-bound}. \qed

\begin{remark}[Why constant probability is the right notion]
\label{rem:constant-probability}
The slack lower bound \eqref{eq:minimax-lower-bound} is a
\emph{one-sided constant-probability} statement: the slack exceeds
$c(\alpha,\kappa) M^2/\sqrt{N-n}$ with probability at least $1/10$.
A high-probability statement of the form $\PR[\cdots] \ge 1-\eta$
with $\eta$ small \emph{cannot} hold for any positive threshold,
because the deviation $\Delta_T$ is exactly centred at $0$ under
$\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ (the upper tail of a centred random variable
carries at most half the probability mass).  This matches the
standard minimax framework of \citet{Tsybakov2009}: a
constant-probability lower bound, combined with a
high-probability upper bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}),
suffices to certify rate-optimality at $\Theta(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$
(Corollary~\ref{cor:minimax-rate-optimality}). Anti-concentration
of the held-out residual is the right tool for the lower side;
concentration of the supremum process is the right tool for the
upper side.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Degeneracy as $\kappa \to 0$ or capacity exceeds $\sqrt{N-n}$]
\label{rem:gamma-degeneracy}
The constant $c(\alpha,\kappa,\eta)$ in
\eqref{eq:minimax-lower-bound} scales as $\sqrt{\kappa}$ and
degenerates in two structural regimes:
\emph{(i)} as $\kappa \to 0$ (every non-trivial vector of
$\Hcal_d^\perp$ has constant squared magnitude $|u_v|^2 \equiv
\mathrm{const}$), in which case the residual energy
$(y^\star_v)^2$ is deterministic across $v$, $\Delta_T \equiv 0$,
and Berry--Esseen anticoncentration genuinely vanishes;
\emph{(ii)} as the capacity $d$ approaches the boundary
$D_0\sqrt{N-n}$ of A\ref{a:capacity}, in which case the leakage
of Lemma~\ref{lem:leakage} approaches the Berry--Esseen anti-%
concentration mass and the union-bound margin collapses. For
$d \gg \sqrt{N-n}$, the oracle leakage genuinely dominates the
Berry--Esseen floor and an in-class oracle can drive $L_{T^c}(\hat
f)$ below $\Var(\by^\star)$; the same regime makes the upper bound
of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} vacuous (since
$\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\Hcal_d) \to \infty$), so the
minimax statement is uninformative regardless of estimator choice.
A\ref{a:incoherence}--A\ref{a:capacity} are therefore not proof
artefacts but the sharp conditions for the adversarial argument
to deliver an $\Omega(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$ slack.
\end{remark}

\section{Corollaries}
\label{sec:corollaries}

\subsection{C1 --- Encoding-gap bound for materials informatics}
\label{subsec:c1}

We compare two learners on the same graph $G$ and target $\by$:
the \emph{categorical one-hot learner} $\Acal_{\mathrm{cat}}$ that
memorises the training labels and predicts the training mean
elsewhere ($\hat f_{\mathrm{cat}}(v) := y_v$ if $v \in T$,
$\hat f_{\mathrm{cat}}(v) := \bar y_T$ if $v \in T^c$), and a
\emph{compositional} ERM learner $\Acal_{\mathrm{comp}}$ on a
fixed closed linear subspace $\Hcal_{\mathrm{comp}} \subseteq \R^N$
of feature-derived predictors.  The encoding-gap claim of MLST
\citep{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound} is that
$\Acal_{\mathrm{cat}}$ is strictly outperformed by
$\Acal_{\mathrm{comp}}$ whenever $\Hcal_{\mathrm{comp}}$ explains
non-trivial variance of $\by$.

\paragraph{Caveat on the framework's applicability to $\Acal_{\mathrm{cat}}$.}
The categorical learner's reachable subspace
$\Scal_{\Acal_{\mathrm{cat}}}(T) := \mathrm{span}(\{\mathbf{e}_v : v \in T\})
+ \mathrm{span}(\bone)$ depends on the random set $T$, violating
the $T$-independence requirement of Definition~\ref{def:learner}.
Hence Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} \emph{does not apply}
to $\Acal_{\mathrm{cat}}$ as stated. We work around this in the
only way the framework permits: we compute
$\E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{cat}})]$ by direct evaluation (no
Bessel projection invoked), and we apply
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} only to the
$T$-independent compositional learner $\Acal_{\mathrm{comp}}$
on $\Hcal_{\mathrm{comp}}$. The encoding-gap claim
\eqref{eq:encoding-gap-upper} is thus a juxtaposition of two
independently valid statements, not a unified corollary, and we
flag it as such for the reader.

\begin{lemma}[Categorical baseline under the proportional regime]
\label{lem:cat-baseline}
Under A\ref{a:proportional} ($n/N = \alpha$ fixed in $[\alpha_{\min},
\alpha_{\max}] \subset (0, 1)$) and a centred target $\by$ with
$\bone^\top \by = 0$ and $\|\by\|_\infty \le M$, the categorical
learner satisfies
\begin{equation}
    \E_{T \sim \Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}}\!\big[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{cat}})\big]
    \;=\; \Var(\by)\;+\;\frac{1}{n}\,\Var(\by)\,(1 + o(1))
    \;=\; \Var(\by)\,(1 + O(1/n)),
    \label{eq:cat-baseline}
\end{equation}
as $N \to \infty$. In particular, the asymptotic baseline is
$\Var(\by)$ for any $\alpha \in (0,1)$, with the leave-one-out
special case $\alpha = (N-1)/N$ recovering the exact prefactor
$(N/(N-1))^2 = 1 + O(1/N)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By symmetry, $\E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{cat}})] = \E_T[(\bar y_T
- y_{v_0})^2 \mid v_0 \in T^c]$ for any fixed $v_0 \in V$.
Conditional on $v_0 \in T^c$, $T$ is a uniform without-replacement
sample of size $n$ from $V \setminus \{v_0\}$, hence
$\E_T[\bar y_T \mid v_0 \in T^c] = (N-1)^{-1}\sum_{v \neq v_0} y_v
= -y_{v_0}/(N-1)$ by centring. The conditional variance of
$\bar y_T$ is
$\Var_T[\bar y_T \mid v_0 \in T^c] = \frac{N-1-n}{n(N-2)}\sigma^2_{v_0}$,
where $\sigma^2_{v_0}$ is the population variance of $\{y_v\}_{v
\neq v_0}$. Expanding,
\begin{align*}
    \E_T[(\bar y_T - y_{v_0})^2 \mid v_0 \in T^c]
    &= \Var_T[\bar y_T \mid v_0 \in T^c]
        + (\E_T[\bar y_T \mid v_0 \in T^c] - y_{v_0})^2 \\
    &= \frac{N-1-n}{n(N-2)}\sigma^2_{v_0}
        + y_{v_0}^2 \cdot \Big(\frac{N}{N-1}\Big)^{\!2}.
\end{align*}
Averaging uniformly over $v_0 \in V$, $N^{-1}\sum_{v_0} y_{v_0}^2 =
\Var(\by)$ and $N^{-1}\sum_{v_0}\sigma^2_{v_0} = \Var(\by)(1 +
O(1/N))$, so
\[
    \E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{cat}})]
    \;=\; \frac{N-1-n}{n(N-2)}\Var(\by)(1 + O(1/N))
        + \Big(\frac{N}{N-1}\Big)^{\!2}\Var(\by).
\]
The first term is $\Var(\by) \cdot \frac{1-\alpha}{\alpha N} (1 +
O(1/N)) = O(1/n)$ under A\ref{a:proportional}, and the second is
$\Var(\by)(1 + O(1/N))$. Combining yields
\eqref{eq:cat-baseline}. At LOOCV ($n = N-1$), the first term
vanishes and the second is exactly $(N/(N-1))^2 \Var(\by)$. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Encoding gap]
\label{cor:encoding-gap}
Under A\ref{a:target}--A\ref{a:proportional} and the hypotheses of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} for $\Acal_{\mathrm{comp}}$
on $\Hcal_{\mathrm{comp}}$, both learners are evaluated under the
same proportional protocol with $\alpha = n/N \in [\alpha_{\min},
\alpha_{\max}]$. The expected held-out gap satisfies
\begin{equation}
    \E_T\!\big[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{cat}})\big]
        \;-\; \E_T\!\big[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{comp}})\big]
    \;\le\; \Rspec(\Hcal_{\mathrm{comp}}, \by)\,\Var(\by)
        \;+\; O\!\big(\Var(\by)/n\big).
    \label{eq:encoding-gap-upper}
\end{equation}
The reverse direction --- a guaranteed structural \emph{lower}
bound on the gap --- additionally requires the upper-bound
saturation guarantee of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} for
$\Acal_{\mathrm{comp}}$, giving
\begin{equation*}
    \E_T\!\big[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{cat}}) - L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{comp}})\big]
    \;\ge\; \Rspec(\Hcal_{\mathrm{comp}}, \by)\,\Var(\by)
        \;-\; \mathrm{slack}_{\mathrm{Thm 2}}(\alpha)
        \;-\; O\!\big(\Var(\by)/n\big),
\end{equation*}
where $\mathrm{slack}_{\mathrm{Thm 2}}(\alpha) = O(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$
under A\ref{a:proportional}, recovering Theorem~1(d) of
\citet{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound} up to the slack of
\eqref{eq:universal-upper-bound}.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
Both terms are evaluated under the same proportional
$\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ protocol with $\alpha = n/N$. Apply
Lemma~\ref{lem:cat-baseline} (with the $O(1/n)$ correction
absorbed into the second term of \eqref{eq:encoding-gap-upper})
to $\E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{cat}})]$, and
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} to
$\E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{comp}})]$; subtract. The
lower-direction statement substitutes the upper bound of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} for $\Acal_{\mathrm{comp}}$,
which is licensed by A\ref{a:proportional}. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Protocol consistency with the empirical validation
of \S\ref{sec:empirics}]
\label{rem:protocol-empirics}
The empirical instantiations in \S\ref{sec:empirics} evaluate both
the categorical and compositional learners under the same
GroupKFold protocol ($\alpha \in \{0.8, 0.9\}$ depending on $k$),
matching the proportional regime of A\ref{a:proportional} that
licenses both Lemma~\ref{lem:cat-baseline} and
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}. The strict LOOCV special
case ($\alpha = (N-1)/N \to 1$) of an earlier draft is recovered
as $k \to N$, but with $\mathrm{slack}_{\mathrm{Thm 2}}(\alpha)$
degrading; we therefore evaluate at a fixed $\alpha \le
\alpha_{\max} = 0.9$ in the empirical panel.
\end{remark}

\subsection{C2 --- Negative transfer (spatial target-orthogonality)}
\label{subsec:c2}

We instantiate the universal framework on the negative-transfer
problem: a learner trained on a \emph{source} task is evaluated on a
\emph{target} task, and we ask under what structural conditions
transfer must fail.  The literature on negative transfer
\citep{Rosenstein2005,WangDaiPoczos2019,Zhang2022survey} formalises
the phenomenon empirically but has not, to our knowledge, provided a
model-free lower bound expressed in the spectral language of the
data-task graph.  C2 fills this gap as a one-page corollary of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}.

\paragraph{Setup.}  Let $G = (V, E)$ be the joint similarity graph on
the union $V = V_{\mathrm{src}} \cup V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ (disjoint
union), with $|V_{\mathrm{src}}| = n_s$, $|V_{\mathrm{tgt}}| = n_t$,
$N := n_s + n_t$.  The graph encodes the chosen task-relatedness
geometry: examples that are jointly close in feature space are
adjacent, whether they belong to the source task, the target task, or
across.  Let $L = \bU \boldsymbol{\Lambda} \bU^\top$ be the
symmetric-normalised Laplacian of $G$ with eigenvectors $\bU = [\bu_1,
\ldots, \bu_N]$.  The joint signal $\by \in \R^N$ is the concatenation
of the source labels $\by_{\mathrm{src}} \in \R^{n_s}$ and the target
labels $\by_{\mathrm{tgt}} \in \R^{n_t}$; each is centred and bounded
($\|\by_{\mathrm{src}}\|_\infty \leq M$,
$\|\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|_\infty \leq M$).

The \emph{leave-task-out protocol} $\Pi_{\mathrm{LTO}}$ is the
\emph{deterministic} split $T = V_{\mathrm{src}}$, $T^c =
V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$.  A \emph{transfer learner} $\Acal$ is an ERM on the
source training set: $\hat f_\Acal := \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d}
L_{V_{\mathrm{src}}}(f)$ where $\Hcal_d$ is the learner's hypothesis
class on $\R^N$ (a linear head on shared features, a foundation-model
embedding's output space, etc.).  The evaluation metric is the
target loss $L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(\hat f_\Acal)$.

\begin{definition}[Negative transfer magnitude]
\label{def:nt-magnitude}
The \emph{negative-transfer magnitude} of the transfer learner $\Acal$
on the source--target pair $(\by_{\mathrm{src}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}})$
is the excess target loss over the in-class optimum on the target:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{NT}(\Acal; \by_{\mathrm{src}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}})
    \;:=\; L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(\hat f_\Acal) - L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(f^\star_{\Hcal_d, \mathrm{tgt}}),
    \label{eq:nt-magnitude}
\end{equation}
where $f^\star_{\Hcal_d, \mathrm{tgt}} := \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d}
L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(f)$ is the target-only ideal predictor in $\Hcal_d$
(unreachable by a source-only learner unless the source and target
populations agree on the projection structure of $\by$).
$\mathrm{NT}(\Acal) > 0$ certifies that transferring source-trained
information \emph{harms} target performance relative to the best
in-class target-only baseline; $\mathrm{NT}(\Acal) = 0$ certifies that
the source-only learner already achieves the in-class target optimum.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Spatial target-orthogonality]
\label{def:target-orthogonality}
Assume $V_{\mathrm{src}}$ is a uniqueness set for $\Hcal_d$. Let
$f_s \in \Hcal_d$ denote the (unique) source in-class minimiser
$\arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d} L_{V_{\mathrm{src}}}(f)$, and let
$\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}} := \bP_{\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}}
\by_{\mathrm{tgt}} \in \R^{n_t}$ denote the (unique) orthogonal
projection of $\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ onto the restriction class
$\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$ in the $\ell_2(V_{\mathrm{tgt}})$
inner product. The pair $(\by_{\mathrm{src}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}})$
is \emph{spatially target-orthogonal relative to $\Hcal_d$} if
\begin{equation}
    \big\langle f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}},\; \pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}
    \big\rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
    \;=\; 0.
    \label{eq:target-orthogonality}
\end{equation}
Note that $\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ is well-defined whether or not
$V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ is a uniqueness set for $\Hcal_d$; in the
few-shot regime $\dim(\Hcal_d) > n_t$, the global lift
$f_t \in \Hcal_d$ with $f_t|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} = \pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}$
is non-unique but the restriction $\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ itself is
unique.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Sufficient spectral condition]
\label{rem:spectral-sufficient}
A clean operational sufficient condition for \eqref{eq:target-orthogonality}
is graph-Fourier disjointness on $\Hcal_d$: if $\Hcal_d \subseteq
\mathrm{span}(\{\bu_k\}_{k \in \mathcal{I}})$ for an index set
$\mathcal{I}$ admitting a partition $\mathcal{I}_s \sqcup
\mathcal{I}_t$ such that $\hat y_{\mathrm{src},k} = \bu_k^\top
\tilde\by_{\mathrm{src}} = 0$ for $k \in \mathcal{I}_t$ and
$\hat y_{\mathrm{tgt},k} = \bu_k^\top \tilde\by_{\mathrm{tgt}} = 0$
for $k \in \mathcal{I}_s$ (zero-padded $\tilde\by$ on the
complementary node set), then $f_s$ and $f_t$ lie in orthogonal
eigen-bands of $\Hcal_d$, and a fortiori $\langle f_s, f_t\rangle = 0$
in $\R^N$. This is the original ``spectral disjointness'' notion
of \citet{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound}; restricting the
inner product to $V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ is in general strictly weaker
(the converse direction requires a sampling-quality argument we do
not invoke), but the weaker target-orthogonality
\eqref{eq:target-orthogonality} is precisely the algebraic
condition needed by Theorem~\ref{thm:negative-transfer-linear-algebra}.
We refer to the property by its target-restricted name throughout
this paper to avoid suggesting equivalence with the global
spectral condition.

Operationally, spectral disjointness expresses that source and
target labels carry information about \emph{different} frequency
modes of the data-task graph: in materials informatics, source and
target properties are driven by structurally orthogonal
compositional trends; in urban mobility, by non-overlapping
commune-cluster eigenmodes. In every case the reading is the same:
\emph{source data carries no $\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$-projectable
information about the target signal}.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Negative transfer under spatial target-orthogonality]
\label{thm:negative-transfer-linear-algebra}
Let $\Hcal_d \subseteq \R^N$ be a closed linear subspace, and
write $\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} := \{f|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} : f
\in \Hcal_d\} \subseteq \R^{n_t}$ for the restriction class on
the target nodes. Suppose:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*),leftmargin=*]
\item $V_{\mathrm{src}}$ is a uniqueness set for $\Hcal_d$,
    i.e., the restriction map $\Hcal_d \to \R^{n_s}$,
    $f \mapsto f|_{V_{\mathrm{src}}}$, is injective. This is the
    standard ERM-on-source identifiability condition and implies
    $\dim(\Hcal_d) \le n_s$;
\item the source--target pair satisfies the spatial
    target-orthogonality condition of
    Definition~\ref{def:target-orthogonality}:
    \[
        \big\langle f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}},\;
            \pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}\big\rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
        \;=\; 0,
    \]
    where $f_s \in \Hcal_d$ is the source ERM (unique by (i)) and
    $\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}} := \bP_{\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}}
    \by_{\mathrm{tgt}} \in \R^{n_t}$ is the orthogonal projection
    of $\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ onto the restriction class
    $\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$.
\end{enumerate}
\textbf{No injectivity is assumed on $V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$.} In
particular, the few-shot regime $\dim(\Hcal_d) > n_t$ is
permitted: $f_t \in \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d}
L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(f)$ may not be unique as a global element of
$\Hcal_d \subseteq \R^N$, but its restriction
$f_t|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} = \pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ is always the
unique projection of $\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ onto
$\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$. The negative-transfer magnitude is
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{NT}(\Acal; \by_{\mathrm{src}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}})
    \;=\; \frac{1}{n_t}\,\big\|
        f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} - \pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}
    \big\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
    \;=\; \frac{1}{n_t}\Big(
        \|f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
        + \|\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
        \Big),
    \label{eq:nt-lower-bound}
\end{equation}
strictly positive whenever $f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} \neq 0$ or
$\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}} \neq 0$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By (i), the source ERM $f_s$ is unique in $\Hcal_d$. Its
restriction $f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$ is therefore unique in
$\R^{n_t}$. The target in-class optimum is, by definition, the
$\ell_2(V_{\mathrm{tgt}})$ orthogonal projection of
$\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ onto $\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$, denoted
$\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}$; this projection is unique in $\R^{n_t}$
\emph{regardless of injectivity} of the restriction map. Any
choice of pre-image $f_t \in \Hcal_d$ with $f_t|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
= \pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ yields the same target loss, so $\mathrm{NT}$
depends only on $f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$ and $\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}$:
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{NT}(\Acal)
    &= L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(f_s) - L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(f_t)\\
    &= \frac{1}{n_t}\Big(
        \|f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} - \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
        - \|\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}} - \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
        \Big)\\
    &= \frac{1}{n_t}\Big(
        \|f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
        - 2\langle f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
        - \|\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
        + 2\langle \pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
        \Big).
\end{align*}
The defining normal equation of $\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ states that
$\by_{\mathrm{tgt}} - \pi_{\mathrm{tgt}} \perp
\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$ in the
$\ell_2(V_{\mathrm{tgt}})$ inner product. Therefore:
(a) $\langle \pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}
    \rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
    = \|\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$ (taking
    inner product with $\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}} \in
    \Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$);
(b) $\langle f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}
    \rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
    = \langle f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}, \pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}
    \rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$ (taking inner product with
    $f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} \in \Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$).
By (ii), $\langle f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}, \pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}
\rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} = 0$. Substituting,
$\mathrm{NT}(\Acal) = n_t^{-1}(
\|f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
+ \|\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}})$,
which is \eqref{eq:nt-lower-bound}. \qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Pure linear-algebra statement; no transduction; few-shot covered]
\label{rem:nt-no-exchangeability}
Theorem~\ref{thm:negative-transfer-linear-algebra} is independent
of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}. The partition
$V = V_{\mathrm{src}} \sqcup V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ is deterministic,
the source ERM is unique under (i), and the proof uses neither
the exchangeability identity~\eqref{eq:exchangeability} nor the
transduction identity (Lemma~\ref{lem:transduction}). The result
holds in the few-shot regime $\dim(\Hcal_d) > n_t$ without
modification: the target projection $\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ is the
unique $\ell_2(V_{\mathrm{tgt}})$ projection of $\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}$
onto $\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$, well-defined regardless of
whether the restriction map $\Hcal_d \to \R^{n_t}$ is injective.
This is a strict generalisation of the prior $d \le \min(n_s,
n_t)$ formulation. The target-orthogonality hypothesis (ii) is
stated directly in $\ell_2(V_{\mathrm{tgt}})$ where it is
actually used, avoiding any appeal to a sampling-quality
extension lemma.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[NT vacuity criterion: spectral alignment]
\label{cor:nt-vacuity}
Conversely, if the source and target signals are \emph{spectrally
aligned} on $\Hcal_d$ --- meaning $f_s|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} =
\pi_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ as elements of $\R^{n_t}$ --- then the
in-class equality $\mathrm{NT}(\Acal) = 0$ holds
\emph{deterministically} at $f_s = f_t$, without any probabilistic
slack: the source-trained ERM coincides with the target in-class
optimum on $V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ as a purely algebraic identity, not
as a high-probability statement. Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}
does \emph{not} apply here (the source/target split is
deterministic, not drawn from $\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$), and no
Rademacher slack carries over.
\end{corollary}

This dichotomy --- vacuous transfer under spectral alignment,
unavoidable harm under spectral disjointness --- localises the
negative-transfer phenomenon to a precise spectral-geometric property
of the (joint-graph, source-signal, target-signal) triple, independent
of the regressor's architecture or training procedure.

\paragraph{Practical check for spatial target-orthogonality.}
In closed-linear $\Hcal_d$ with orthonormal basis $\bB \in \R^{N
\times d}$, the target-orthogonality
condition~\eqref{eq:target-orthogonality} is checkable in
$O(d^2 N)$ time by computing $\langle f_s, f_t\rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$
directly. The (strictly stronger) graph-Fourier sufficient condition
of Remark~\ref{rem:spectral-sufficient} adds the spectral diagnostic:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*),leftmargin=*]
\item Build the joint similarity graph $G$ on $V = V_{\mathrm{src}}
    \cup V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ and compute the bottom-$K$ eigenvectors
    $\bu_1, \ldots, \bu_K$ of its Laplacian (for some practitioner-chosen
    bandlimit $K$; typically $K = O(\dim \Hcal_d \cdot \log N)$).
\item Compute the in-class projections
    $\bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{src}}$ and
    $\bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ (zero-padded outside their
    respective node sets) and their Fourier coefficients
    $\hat y_{\mathrm{src}, k}, \hat y_{\mathrm{tgt}, k}$ for
    $k \leq K$.
\item Test whether $|\hat y_{\mathrm{src}, k} \cdot \hat y_{\mathrm{tgt}, k}|
    \leq \epsilon$ for all $k \leq K$ and some chosen tolerance
    $\epsilon$. If yes, the pair satisfies the spectral sufficient
    condition, hence target-orthogonality, hence
    Theorem~\ref{thm:negative-transfer-linear-algebra} predicts
    unavoidable negative transfer.
\end{enumerate}
Computationally, this is the same cost as the spectral $R^2$
computation already standard for Corollary~C1 evaluation
\citep{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound}, applied to the union
graph instead of the single-domain graph.

\paragraph{Empirical validation programme.}  The corollary is amenable
to empirical falsification in any setting where two related supervised
tasks share a graph: paired MatBench tasks (e.g.,
\texttt{mp\_e\_form} $\to$ \texttt{mp\_gap}) on the composition graph,
QM9-derived molecular properties on shared structure graphs, or
mode-choice models trained in one French commune cluster and evaluated
on another in the \texttt{mobility-applicability-bound} panel.
A dedicated empirical pre-registration is in preparation as the
companion repository \texttt{negative-transfer-corollary} of the
\texttt{cycling-data-lab} organisation.

\subsection{C3 --- Active learning: out of scope, deferred to companion work}
\label{subsec:c3}

The active-learning regime --- in which the learner \emph{chooses}
$T \subseteq V$ via a sampling distribution $\pi : V \to [0,1]$
adapted to the spectral geometry of $\Hcal_d$
\citep{Settles2009,Hanneke2014,GuilloryBilmes2009,GaddeAnisOrtega2014,DrineasMahoney2018,Avron2017,Puy2018}
--- is a natural successor to Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}.
We make explicit here that it is \emph{not} a corollary of the present
framework, and we record what would be required to obtain one.

\paragraph{Why spatial exchangeability is load-bearing.}
The proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}
(Section~\ref{sec:lower-bound-proof}) uses the protocol only through
the identity
\begin{equation}
    \E_{T \sim \Dcal_\Pi}[L_T(f)] \;=\; L_V(f) \qquad
    \text{for every deterministic } f \in \R^N,
    \label{eq:exchangeability}
\end{equation}
which holds for $\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ because the marginal inclusion
probability $\PR_T[v \in T]$ is the constant $n/N$, independent of
$v$. Under any non-uniform protocol with $\PR_T[v \in T] = \pi(v)$
non-constant, identity~\eqref{eq:exchangeability} fails for the
unweighted $L_T$:
\begin{equation*}
    \E_T[L_T(f)] \;=\; \frac{1}{\E_T[|T|]} \sum_{v \in V} \pi(v)\,(f(v) - y_v)^2
    \;\neq\; L_V(f) \quad \text{in general,}
\end{equation*}
and the cancellation between the Bessel floor on $L_V$
(Lemma~\ref{lem:bessel}) and the ERM inequality on $L_T$
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:lower-step3-LT}), which produces the
\emph{exact-in-expectation} statement of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}, is destroyed. The bound
becomes either vacuous or strictly weaker, depending on the sign of
$\Cov_v\!\big(\pi(v), (f(v) - y_v)^2\big)$.

\paragraph{What an active-learning corollary would require.}
Restoring~\eqref{eq:exchangeability} under a non-uniform $\pi$ forces
replacing $L_T$ by the Horvitz--Thompson / importance-weighted
training risk
\begin{equation}
    L_T^\pi(f) \;:=\; \frac{1}{N}\sum_{v \in T}\frac{(f(v) - y_v)^2}{\pi(v)},
    \qquad \E_T[L_T^\pi(f)] = L_V(f),
    \label{eq:hto-risk}
\end{equation}
which is unbiased \emph{by construction}. This change, however,
breaks the symmetry with the unweighted held-out risk $L_{T^c}$ that
the practitioner actually evaluates: the transduction identity of
Lemma~\ref{lem:transduction} no longer admits constants
$(\alpha, \beta)$ such that
$L_V(f) = \alpha L_T^\pi(f) + \beta L_{T^c}(f)$.
A correct weighted transduction identity involves the
\emph{second-order} inclusion probabilities
$\pi_{vw} := \PR_T[v \in T, w \in T]$ and the residual
covariance structure on $T^c$, and is non-trivial. The expected
rate, derivable from the spectral-graph A-optimal design literature
\citep{DrineasMahoney2018,Avron2017,Puy2018}, is the standard
active-learning improvement $\widetilde O(M^2 \sqrt{d/n} \cdot
\log(1/\eta))$ at leverage-score-optimal $\pi$.

\paragraph{Status.}
A rigorous extension of Theorems~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}--\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}
to importance-weighted protocols requires: (i) a weighted
Bessel--Parseval identity that controls $L_V$ in terms of
$L_T^\pi$ and a $\pi$-dependent residual; (ii) a Horvitz--Thompson
analogue of the transduction identity, calibrated by second-order
inclusions; (iii) a Berry--Esseen anticoncentration on
$\pi$-weighted sums without replacement to match
Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}. None of these are present in
this manuscript, and we do not claim an active-learning corollary.
A dedicated treatment is in preparation as the companion paper
``Importance-Weighted Transduction and Active Learning on Graphs''
of the \texttt{cycling-data-lab} programme, and is recorded as Open
Question~(2) in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

\subsection{Discussion of the cross-domain anchors}
\label{subsec:emp-cross-domain}

The cross-domain pilots provide a methodological check that the
framework is not specific to materials informatics.  Three substantive
patterns are visible in Table~\ref{tab:cross-domain}.

\paragraph{The negative-$\Delta R^2$ regime is recoverable.}  The
MovieLens row exhibits $\Delta R^2_{\mathrm{LSO}} = -0.398$:
\emph{the categorical (item-ID) encoder outperforms the
compositional (item-feature) encoder under leave-item-out}.  By
Corollary~C1, this is exactly the prediction when $\Rspec(\Hcal_d,
\by) \approx 0$, i.e., the chosen feature subspace is structurally
\emph{orthogonal} to the target signal.  In the MovieLens case, the
item-feature subspace (genre indicators, year, popularity) is
predictive in-sample but fails to span the user-preference
eigenmodes of the rating matrix; the LSO gap is correctly negative.
The MatBench \texttt{matbench\_dielectric} row exhibits the same
pattern ($\Delta R^2 = -0.112$ with $\Rspec = 0.20$), again
consistent with the bound.

\paragraph{Encoder discrimination on shared substrate.}  On the same
8 MatBench tasks, the framework discriminates between encoders by
their $\Rspec$ value.  Replacing the Magpie compositional encoder by
the CHGNet foundation-model crystal embedding
\citep{Deng2023chgnet} on \texttt{matbench\_mp\_gap} reduces the
tail energy by $\Delta \epsilon_K = -0.13$ ($24$ pp; see d24 of
\texttt{materials-applicability-bound}), translating directly to a
larger $\Rspec$ and a tighter lower-bound floor.  This is the
predicted behaviour: a richer feature space increases $\Rspec(\Hcal_d,
\by)$ and tightens the bound on the held-out loss.

\paragraph{Replication in unrelated domains.}  The QSAR pilot
(molecular activity, $\Delta R^2 = 0.612$) and the AFLOW pilots
(bulk and shear moduli, $\Delta R^2 \approx 0.84$) replicate the
qualitative pattern on supervised regression problems with entirely
different physical content (chemoinformatics, ab-initio
solid-state).  Quantitative $\Rspec$ values for these pilots have
not been computed (they require constructing a domain-appropriate
similarity graph, which is non-trivial for QSAR molecules and
which we have not pre-registered); however, the consistent direction
and magnitude of $\Delta R^2$ in domains where $\Rspec$ is
\emph{expected} to be high provides directional confirmation.

\section{Discussion and open questions}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the bound says, and what it does not}

Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} establishes that the expected
leave-node-out error of any learner is bounded below by the irreducible
component $(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)) \Var(\by)$.  This quantity depends
only on three things: the population variance of the target, the
hypothesis class of the learner, and the projection of the target onto
that class.  It does \emph{not} depend on the regressor's training
algorithm, the choice of optimiser, the random seed, or the
hyperparameters --- all of which can only affect how closely the slack
of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} is achieved.

Equivalently, the bound says: \emph{there is no algorithmic trick to
beat the structural floor}.  If your hypothesis class explains $40\%$
of the population variance, no choice of optimiser, regulariser, or
ensembling strategy can drive the expected leave-node-out error below
$60\%$ of the target variance.  Reducing the floor requires changing
the hypothesis class, not the algorithm.

\subsection{Two senses of informativeness: typical-case and worst-case}
\label{subsec:two-senses}

The framework's bounds operate in two distinct informativeness
regimes that should not be conflated, and \emph{neither} of them
is a deployment-time forecasting tool in the strict sense.

\paragraph{Diagnostic / retrospective use.}  In the typical
operational regime, the practitioner has access to $\by$
in full (the experimentally measured property values, the
observed mode shares, the realised user ratings) and uses the
framework \emph{retrospectively}: given the hypothesis class
$\Hcal_d$ of the deployed learner, compute the spectral $R^2$
$\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)$ and compare the predicted floor
$(1 - \Rspec)\Var(\by)$ to the empirically observed
leave-node-out error.  The framework's value here is
\emph{structural diagnosis}: if the observed error is much higher
than the floor, the gap localises a deficiency of optimisation
or regularisation; if it is at the floor, no algorithmic
improvement can close it without changing $\Hcal_d$.

\paragraph{Falsification / worst-case use.}  Independently,
Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound} provides a
\emph{universal} (target-independent) lower bound: for any
$\Hcal_d$, there \emph{exists} an adversarial $\by^\star$ on
which the held-out error of any learner exceeds the floor by
$\Omega(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$.  This is a \emph{falsification} bound:
any algorithmic claim of beating the floor uniformly across
targets is structurally impossible.

\paragraph{Why this is not a forecasting tool.}  We emphasise that
the framework is \emph{not} a deployment-time predictor of
generalisation error in the strict sense. Computing the floor
$(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)$ requires $\by$ on the full
node set $V$ --- the very quantity a forecasting tool would attempt
to predict. The framework is therefore a tool for
\emph{retrospective structural diagnosis} (after labels are
observed) and \emph{worst-case falsification} (universal lower
bound via Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}), \emph{not} a
substitute for held-out evaluation. Forecasting applications
(``how much error should I expect on the next, as-yet-unlabelled
deployment?'') require a separate prior or extrapolation model on
$\by$ --- e.g., a related-task posterior, a generative model of
typical signals on $\Hcal_d$, or a transfer-learning bound on the
$\Rspec$ of a target signal given a source --- and lie outside
the deterministic-target framework of this paper. We make this
operational distinction explicit because an earlier draft of
\S\ref{subsec:two-senses} elided it; the bound's
\emph{informativeness} on labelled retrospective data is logically
distinct from its (absent) \emph{informativeness} on an unlabelled
future deployment.

\subsection{Open questions remaining}
\label{subsec:open}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*),leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Sharp matching constant beyond rate match.}  Upper
    and lower bounds match in rate ($1/\sqrt{N-n}$) after the
    transductive Rademacher upgrade
    (\texttt{notes/04\_sharp\_transductive\_constant.tex}).
    The \emph{residual} constant gap is
    $\mathrm{gap}(\alpha, \kappa) \propto 1/\sqrt{\alpha \kappa}$
    (Corollary~\ref{cor:minimax-rate-optimality},
    Eq.~\eqref{eq:cramer-rao-gap}): at the operational midpoint
    $\alpha = 0.5$, $\kappa \approx 0.25$, the gap is $\approx
    194$, where the upper-side constant $23.1 = 20.2 + 2.83$
    aggregates the \citet[][Thm.~1]{ElYanivPechyony2006}
    Rademacher constant ($20.2 = 5.05 \cdot 4$, post-loss-range
    rescaling) and the Serfling slack absorption ($2.83 =
    4/\sqrt{2}$, see proof of
    Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}). The lower-side
    constant $\tau/2 = \Phi^{-1}(3/4)/2 \approx 0.337$ is fixed
    independent of $\eta$, since the lower bound is a
    \emph{constant-probability} anti-concentration statement at
    $c' \ge 1/10$ rather than a high-probability statement
    (Remark~\ref{rem:constant-probability}).  Each of the two
    upper-side concentration sub-steps (EYP and Serfling) can be
    individually tightened via the without-replacement Bernstein
    refinement of \citet[][Cor.~2.5]{BardenetMaillard2015}: the
    EYP factor $20.2 \to \sqrt 2 \cdot 4 \approx 5.66$ and the
    Serfling factor $2.83 \to \sqrt 2 \approx 1.41$, giving an
    aggregated upper-side constant $\approx 7.07$ and a midpoint
    gap $\approx 59$.  The gap is bounded but not universal: it
    scales as $1/\sqrt{\alpha}$, so a 95/5 split ($\alpha = 0.95$)
    tightens it further while the few-shot regime $\alpha \to 0$
    degrades it without bound.  A tighter leakage bound (e.g.\ via
    the operator-Bernstein refinement of \citealp{Tropp2015} or a
    $\chi^2$-tail concentration instead of Markov) would shave
    the leakage budget and bring the midpoint gap closer to $30$
    post-Bardenet--Maillard.  A previously conjectured factor-$4$
    improvement via a multi-coordinate Fano / Assouad
    construction is \emph{not} achievable: the Berry--Esseen rate
    originates from the anticoncentration of
    $|T^c|^{-1}\sum_{T^c} X_v$ on real-valued residuals, whereas
    Assouad's lemma on the Dirac observations of the
    leave-node-out protocol reduces to binary coordinate-wise
    tests whose total-variation distance is in $\{0, 1\}$, with
    no $1/\sqrt{N-n}$-scale intermediate sensitivity to exploit
    (\texttt{notes/07}~Proposition~6 in the referenced notes).
    The sharp matching constant at the operational midpoint is
    thus $\approx 59$, not $1$.

\item \textbf{Importance-weighted transduction identity.}  Extending
    Lemma~\ref{lem:transduction} to non-uniform protocols (e.g.,
    leverage-score sampling for active learning) requires an
    importance-weighted variant of \eqref{eq:transduction-identity}.
    Mechanical, deferred to the C3 companion paper.

\item \textbf{Approximate ERM.}  Assumption~\ref{a:erm} requires
    strict ERM.  For an $\epsilon_T$-suboptimal ERM
    ($L_T(\hat f_\Acal) \le \inf_{f \in \Hcal_d} L_T(f) +
    \epsilon_T$, with $\epsilon_T \ge 0$ a $T$-measurable
    integrable random variable), the lower bound of
    Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} acquires an additive
    slack of exactly $(\rho(\Pi) - 1)\, \E_T[\epsilon_T]$
    (\texttt{notes/07\_four\_axes\_extensions.tex}~Theorem~2).
    Calibration: for ridge at optimal regularisation,
    $\E_T[\epsilon_T] \asymp \sigma_\xi^2 d/n$ giving slack
    $\approx 4\sigma_\xi^2 d/n$ at $\rho = 5$; for $K$-round
    early-stopped GBM, slack $\approx 4\|f^\star\|^2/K$; for
    SGD-trained NN in the NTK regime, exponentially small.

\item \textbf{Misspecified $\Hcal_d$ at deployment.}  The bound applies
    to the actual reachable class $\Hcal_d$ of the deployed learner,
    not to the modeller's nominal description of it.  For learners
    with explicit implicit-bias tie-breaking $\Omega$ (e.g., ridge,
    Lasso, min-norm reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS)
    interpolation), Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}
    applies verbatim with $\Hcal_d$ replaced by the $\Omega$-effective
    reachable subspace $\Scal_\Acal^\Omega$ defined as the closure
    of the learner's image
    (\texttt{notes/07\_four\_axes\_extensions.tex}~Theorem~10).
    For the most operationally important sub-case --- min-norm RKHS
    interpolation under a strictly positive-definite kernel
    (neural tangent kernel (NTK) regime, over-parameterised neural
    network) --- this reformulation is
    operationally trivial since $\Scal_\Acal^\Omega \subseteq
    \mathrm{range}(\bK) = \R^N$. A non-trivial floor in this regime,
    via a transductive adaptation of the
    \citet{BartlettLongLugosiTsigler2020} benign-overfitting analysis,
    is an \emph{open problem}; the research programme is laid out in
    \texttt{notes/08\_benign\_overfitting\_research\_programme.tex}
    as three conjectures parameterised by the missing technical
    ingredients (matrix Chernoff for principal submatrices sampled
    without replacement; exchangeable-martingale concentration for
    $T$-dependent trace functionals; correct RKHS-vs-$\ell_2$ norm
    conversion under spectral truncation).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{subsec:limitations}

\paragraph{Stochastic targets: clean noise relaxation.}
The framework's primary results are stated for a
\emph{deterministic} target $\by$. The stochastic-target extension
is not an open question: under additive independent zero-mean
\emph{homoscedastic} noise $\by^{\mathrm{obs}} = \by + \bxi$ with
$\Var(\xi_v) = \sigma_\xi^2$ \emph{for every $v \in V$}, and a
linear ERM learner whose hypothesis class
\emph{(including all hyperparameters)} is chosen independently of
$\bxi$, the structural floor of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} relaxes by exactly
\begin{equation}
    -\,\frac{2\, d_{\mathrm{eff}}\, \sigma_\xi^2}{N - n},
    \label{eq:noise-relaxation}
\end{equation}
where $d_{\mathrm{eff}} := \dim \Hcal_d$. The homoscedasticity
hypothesis is essential: under heteroscedastic noise with
per-node variance $\sigma_v^2$, the cross-term $\E[\langle
\bP_{\Hcal_d}\bxi, \bxi\rangle]$ becomes
$\tr(\bP_{\Hcal_d}\,\mathrm{Diag}(\sigma_v^2))$, which can exceed
$d_{\mathrm{eff}} \sigma_\xi^2$ by an arbitrary factor when
$\Hcal_d$ concentrates on high-variance nodes. This is
Theorem~1 of
\texttt{notes/07\_four\_axes\_extensions.tex} and exactly recovers
the in-sample noise variance of ordinary least squares (OLS) at fixed
design; the
operational reading of the floor as
``signal-plus-noise-explainable variance'' is preserved up to the
$2 d_{\mathrm{eff}} \sigma_\xi^2/(N-n)$ correction. For Gaussian
noise and a (possibly non-linear) weakly differentiable estimator,
Stein's unbiased risk estimate (SURE) replaces $d_{\mathrm{eff}}$ by
the Efron
degrees of freedom $\E_{\bxi}[\df(\Acal, T)]$
(\texttt{notes/07}~Theorem~1$'$-Stein). For cross-validated
hyperparameter selection, an additional model-selection-stability
term of order $\sigma_\xi^2 \E_{\bxi}[\Delta_{\bxi}
d_{\mathrm{eff}}]/n$ applies (\texttt{notes/07}~Remark~3); we
recommend pre-registering the hyperparameter grid to control it.

\paragraph{Model selection.}
The framework does not address the choice of $\Hcal_d$ itself.
Selecting $\Hcal_d$ to maximise $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)$ subject to a
fixed Rademacher complexity is a separate problem (essentially
representation learning), on which the bound only comments
indirectly via the trade-off between $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)$ and
$\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\Hcal_d)$ in the slack of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}. A data-adaptive selector
that picks $\Hcal_d$ post-hoc on the same $(T, \by|_T)$ used to fit
the learner invalidates the data-independence required for
\eqref{eq:noise-relaxation} and falls outside its scope.

\paragraph{Misspecification at deployment.}
The bound applies to the actual reachable class $\Hcal_d$ of the
deployed learner, not to the modeller's nominal description of it.
For learners with explicit implicit-bias tie-breaking $\Omega$
(ridge, Lasso, min-norm RKHS interpolation),
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} applies verbatim with
$\Hcal_d$ replaced by the $\Omega$-effective reachable subspace
$\Scal_\Acal^\Omega$ (\texttt{notes/07}~Theorem~10). The strictly
non-trivial sub-case --- a meaningful floor for min-norm RKHS
interpolation on a strictly-PD kernel, where $\Scal_\Acal^\Omega
\subseteq \mathrm{range}(\bK) = \R^N$ --- remains open and is laid
out as the research programme of
\texttt{notes/08\_benign\_overfitting\_research\_programme.tex}.

\subsection{Conclusion}
\label{subsec:conclusion}

We have established three theorems characterising the
graph-supervised leave-node-out regression problem under the
proportional regime $n = \alpha N$.
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} provides the structural
floor $(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)$, exact in expectation
and independent of the regressor's training procedure.
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} exhibits an empirical-risk
minimiser that saturates the floor up to a transductive-Rademacher
slack of order $M^2/\sqrt{N - n}$.
Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound} proves that this slack is
minimax-tight in rate (one-sided, constant-probability anti-%
concentration with $c' \ge 1/10$) under the dispersion assumption
A\ref{a:incoherence} and the leverage-incoherence capacity
hypothesis A\ref{a:capacity}, with the residual multiplicative-%
constant gap $\mathrm{gap}(\alpha, \kappa) \approx 59$ at the
operational midpoint $\alpha = 0.5$, $\kappa \approx 0.25$ after
the Bardenet--Maillard tightening
(Corollary~\ref{cor:minimax-rate-optimality}).
The pair of bounds is the Cram\'er--Rao analog of the classical
efficiency inequality for parameter estimation, and the empirical
panel of Section~\ref{sec:empirics} validates the bound on the
eight-task MatBench panel with Spearman $\rho(\Rspec, \Delta
R^2_{\mathrm{LSO}}) = +1.000$ ($n = 8$, exact permutation $p = 4.96
