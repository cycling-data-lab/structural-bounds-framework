\begin{abstract}
We prove a universal lower bound on the expected leave-node-out
generalisation error of any feature-restricted learner on any
graph-supervised regression problem.  The bound depends only on three
objects: the graph Laplacian, the target signal, and the learner's
reachable feature subspace.  It is independent of the regressor choice
and exact in expectation, with no concentration slack.  We prove that
an empirical-risk-minimising learner saturates the bound to a
slack of order $M^2/\sqrt{N - n}$ via the transductive Rademacher
complexity of \citet{ElYanivPechyony2006}, and that this slack is
itself minimax-tight: even an oracle estimator with full knowledge
of the target on the population cannot drive it lower.
The pair of bounds is the Cram\'er--Rao analog, for graph-supervised
learning under leave-node-out evaluation, of the classical efficiency
inequality for parameter estimation.  Four corollaries instantiate the
framework: the encoding-gap bound for materials-property prediction
(recovering~\citealp{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound}), a spectral-disjointness
bound on negative transfer, a label-complexity dual for active learning,
and an empirical validation across the eight-task MatBench panel,
27-network bike-share demand panel, and 34{,}858-commune French mobility
panel established by the companion repositories of the cycling-data-lab
organisation.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} graph-supervised learning; generalisation
lower bound; leave-node-out cross-validation; spectral methods; Laplacian
eigenfunctions; Berry--Esseen anticoncentration; Cram\'er--Rao analog;
applicability domain; transfer learning; active learning.

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Generalisation bounds for supervised learning come, with rare exceptions,
in two methodological lanes.  The VC / Rademacher lane
\citep{Vapnik1998,Mohri2018book,Tripuraneni2020} bounds the gap between
empirical and population risk in terms of the hypothesis class's
combinatorial or geometric complexity, yielding \emph{upper bounds} on
the excess risk.  The information-theoretic lane
\citep{Russo2016,Xu2017,Tran2019,Nguyen2023} bounds the same gap in terms
of the mutual information between the training data and the learned
hypothesis, again yielding upper bounds.  In both cases, the resulting
inequalities tell us how \emph{badly} a learner could perform; they do
not characterise an irreducible floor below which no learner can go.

In a parallel development at the intersection of graph signal processing
\citep{Shuman2013,Ortega2018} and statistical learning, the question of
\emph{leave-node-out generalisation} on a graph-supported regression
problem has acquired particular significance.  The materials-informatics
community has investigated the \emph{applicability-domain problem}
\citep{Tropsha2010,Sahigara2012,Hanser2016} --- the empirical observation
that models trained on one family of compositions generalise poorly to
materials outside that family --- through largely empirical means.  The
recommender-systems community has investigated the \emph{cold-start
problem} \citep{Schein2002,Marlin2003,Kluver2014} as a structurally
similar question on user--item graphs.  Urban mobility research has
investigated the failure of mode-choice models trained in one city to
generalise to others \citep{Naik2014,Hillel2021}.  In every case the
empirical phenomenology is unambiguous, but a quantitative theorem
characterising the \emph{floor} of the held-out error --- as a function
of the data, not of the regressor --- was, until recently, missing.

The companion paper \citet{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound} provided one
such floor, specifically for the materials-informatics setting.  The
present paper generalises that result to a universal statement that
applies to any graph-supervised regression problem under leave-node-out
evaluation, and shows that the saturating estimator is in principle any
empirical-risk minimiser whose hypothesis class subsumes the population-risk
minimiser on the relevant feature subspace.  Together with a matching
Berry--Esseen minimax lower bound, this yields a Cram\'er--Rao-style
efficiency statement for graph-supervised learning.

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*),leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Universal lower bound
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}).}  For any learner $\Acal$
    with reachable hypothesis class $\Hcal_d$, target $\by \in \R^N$
    with $\|\by\|_\infty \leq M$, and uniform leave-node-out protocol
    $\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ with $|T| = n$, the expected leave-node-out loss
    satisfies
    \begin{equation*}
        \E_T\!\big[L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))\big] \;\geq\; \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)\big) \cdot \Var(\by),
    \end{equation*}
    where $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by) := 1 - L_V(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})/\Var(\by)$
    and $f^\star_{\Hcal_d} := \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d} L_V(f)$.  The
    bound is \emph{exact in expectation}: no concentration slack
    appears.  Proved as
    Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} in
    Section~\ref{sec:lower-bound-proof}.

\item \textbf{Universal upper bound
    (Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}).}  Any empirical-risk-minimising
    learner with truncated $L$-Lipschitz hypothesis class $\Hcal_d$ of
    transductive Rademacher complexity $\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\Hcal_d)$
    \citep{ElYanivPechyony2006} satisfies, with probability $\geq 1 -
    \eta$ over $T \sim \Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$,
    \begin{equation*}
        L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))
        \;\leq\; \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)\big) \cdot \Var(\by)
            + 2 \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\ell \circ \Hcal_d)
            + 5.05\, M^2 \sqrt{\tfrac{(n + u)\log(2/\eta)}{n u}},
    \end{equation*}
    where $u := N - n$ is the held-out cardinality and $\ell$ is the
    truncated square loss.  The slack is $O(M^2/\sqrt{N - n})$,
    matching the Berry--Esseen lower bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}
    in rate.  Proved in Section~\ref{sec:upper-bound-proof}.

\item \textbf{Minimax tightness (Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}).}
    No estimator --- including an oracle with full population knowledge
    of $\by$ --- can drive the slack of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}
    below $\Omega(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$ in the worst case over signals.  The
    argument is a Berry--Esseen anticoncentration on the residual energy
    $\|\by^\star_\perp\|^2_{T^c}$, the fluctuation of which is intrinsic
    to the random test set, not estimator-dependent.  Proved in
    Section~\ref{sec:minimax-proof}.

\item \textbf{Cram\'er--Rao analogy.}  Theorems
    \ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}--\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound} are
    the graph-supervised-learning analog of the classical efficiency
    inequality $\Var(\hat\theta) \geq 1/I(\theta)$:
    Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} provides a structural
    floor on the expected loss depending only on (graph, target,
    learner's reach), and Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}
    exhibits an explicit efficient estimator achieving the floor up to a
    minimax-tight slack.  The structural role of the Fisher information
    is played by the variance of the residual energy
    $((\bP_{\Hcal_d^\perp}\by)(v))^2$ on $V$.

\item \textbf{Corollaries.}
    \begin{itemize}[label=$\bullet$,leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{C1 (encoding-gap recovery; \S\ref{subsec:c1}).}  The
        bound of \citet{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound} on the categorical
        vs.\ compositional encoding gap under leave-composition-out
        evaluation is a one-paragraph corollary, with the categorical
        learner the degenerate case $\Hcal_d = \{$constants$\}$ and
        the compositional learner the case $\Hcal_d = \Scal_{\mathrm{comp}}$.
    \item \textbf{C2 (negative transfer; \S\ref{subsec:c2}).}  Taking
        $V = V_{\mathrm{src}} \cup V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ and $\Pi$ the
        leave-task-out protocol, the bound recovers a structural
        \emph{spectral-disjointness} criterion for negative transfer:
        transfer is unavoidably harmful when the source and target
        labels project onto disjoint bands of the joint graph's
        Laplacian eigenbasis.
    \item \textbf{C3 (active learning; \S\ref{subsec:c3}).}  Letting the
        protocol $\Pi$ be chosen by the learner gives an
        importance-weighted variant in which the optimal sampling
        distribution is the leverage-score distribution on the learner's
        reachable subspace, with the bound matching the rate of the
        spectral-graph A-optimal design literature
        \citep{DrineasMahoney2018,Avron2017}.
    \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Empirical validation.}  The companion repositories
    \texttt{materials-applicability-bound}
    \citep{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound} and
    \texttt{mobility-applicability-bound} (Foss\'e and Pallares, in
    preparation) provide cross-domain empirical instantiations on the
    8-task MatBench panel, 27-network bike-share demand panel, and
    34{,}858-commune French mobility panel respectively.
    Section~\ref{sec:empirics} summarises the headline results and
    points to the per-repository scripts for reproduction.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Relation to prior work}
\label{subsec:related-work}

The four nearest methodological lanes are:

\textbf{(a) Rademacher / VC bounds.}  Classical generalisation bounds
\citep{Vapnik1998,Mohri2018book} and the modern transfer-learning
extensions \citep{Tripuraneni2020,Du2020} give \emph{upper} bounds on
excess risk via Rademacher complexity, eluder dimension, or task
diversity.  Our Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} reuses the
Rademacher technology to prove its upper bound, but the principal
contribution is the matching lower bound and the resulting
Cram\'er--Rao-style efficiency statement, which has no analog in the
VC lane.

\textbf{(b) Information-theoretic bounds.}  Recent
information-theoretic bounds on transferability
\citep{Tran2019,Nguyen2023} and on generalisation
\citep{Russo2016,Xu2017} provide model-free upper bounds via conditional
entropy or PAC-Bayes.  Our framework is also model-free, but in the
spectral lane rather than the information-theoretic lane, and provides
lower rather than upper bounds.

\textbf{(c) Graph signal processing on bandlimited subspaces.}  The
classical bandlimited reconstruction theorems of
\citet{Pesenson2008,AnisGaddeOrtega2016} and the recent generalisation
to arbitrary feature subspaces by \citet{ChepuriLeus2018,TanakaEldar2020}
provide the underlying sampling-theoretic machinery for graph signals
restricted to a subspace.  Our framework borrows the notion of a
subspace-restricted estimator but, crucially, does \emph{not} require
the Pesenson sampling quality $\sigma_T(\Hcal_d)$ to appear in the
bound; see Remark~\ref{rem:no-sigma-T-needed} for the resolution of an
intermediate misconception in our own development.

\textbf{(d) Cycling-data-lab structural-bounds program.}  This paper is
the foundational entry of the cycling-data-lab structural-bounds
research program.  It absorbs and unifies the empirical
applicability-domain bound of \citet{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound} (MLST,
materials informatics) and the mobility-transfer instantiation in
preparation by the same authors (TR-B, urban mobility).  The bound
established here is the \emph{floor}; the empirical companions
demonstrate that the floor is operationally tight in two unrelated
domains.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Graph, signal, and spectrum}

Let $G = (V, E)$ be a finite undirected graph with $|V| = N$.  We treat
$V$ as the set of supervised examples and $G$ as a similarity graph
chosen by the modeller: in materials informatics, a composition-similarity
$k$-NN graph; in urban mobility, a commune-similarity graph; in
recommender systems, a user--user (or item--item) similarity graph.

The symmetric-normalised Laplacian is
$\Lsym = \bI - \bD^{-1/2} \bW \bD^{-1/2}$, where $\bW$ is the
weighted adjacency matrix and $\bD$ the diagonal degree matrix.  Its
eigendecomposition is $\Lsym = \bU \boldsymbol{\Lambda} \bU^\top$ with
$0 = \lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \cdots \leq \lambda_N \leq 2$ and
$\bU = [\bu_1, \ldots, \bu_N]$ orthonormal.

The target signal is a vector $\by \in \R^N$, treated as deterministic
(not random).  Without loss of generality we assume $\by$ is centred,
$\bone^\top \by = 0$ (this absorbs any constant predictor into the
reachable subspace; see Remark~\ref{rem:centring}).  Under this
convention, the population variance
$\Var(\by) := N^{-1} \|\by\|^2$.
We assume the signal is bounded: $\|\by\|_\infty \leq M < \infty$.

The graph Fourier coefficients of $\by$ are $\hat y_k := \bu_k^\top \by$,
so $\by = \sum_k \hat y_k \bu_k$ and $\|\by\|^2 = \sum_k \hat y_k^2$
by Parseval.

\subsection{Evaluation protocol}
\label{subsec:setup-protocol}

\begin{definition}[Evaluation protocol]
\label{def:protocol}
An \emph{evaluation protocol} is a distribution $\Dcal_\Pi$ over
ordered pairs $(T, T^c)$ with $T \subset V$, $|T| = n$,
$T^c := V \setminus T$, $|T^c| = N - n$.  The canonical instance
$\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ samples $T$ uniformly without replacement.
\end{definition}

For a predictor $\hat f : V \to \R$ (identified with its vector of
node predictions in $\R^N$), we write
$L_T(\hat f) := n^{-1} \sum_{v \in T} (\hat f(v) - y_v)^2$,
$L_{T^c}(\hat f) := (N-n)^{-1} \sum_{v \in T^c} (\hat f(v) - y_v)^2$,
and $L_V(\hat f) := N^{-1} \sum_{v \in V} (\hat f(v) - y_v)^2$.
The \emph{transduction factor} is $\rho(\Pi) := N/(N-n)$; for the
standard 80/20 split, $\rho = 5$.

\subsection{Learner and reachable hypothesis class}
\label{subsec:setup-learner}

\begin{definition}[Learner]
\label{def:learner}
A \emph{learner} is a (possibly randomised) map $\Acal : (T, \by|_T)
\mapsto \hat f_\Acal(T) \in \Hcal_d$, where $\Hcal_d \subseteq \R^N$ is
the \emph{hypothesis class}: the set of vector-valued predictors the
algorithm can output, identified with their restrictions to $V$.  We
assume $\Hcal_d$ is closed under truncation to $[-M, M]$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Learner-specific spectral $R^2$]
\label{def:learner-rspec}
Let $f^\star_{\Hcal_d} := \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d} L_V(f)$ be the
\emph{population-risk minimiser} of $\Hcal_d$ (which exists by
compactness under truncation).  The \emph{learner-specific spectral
$R^2$} of $\Hcal_d$ on $\by$ is
\begin{equation}
    \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by) \;:=\; 1 \,-\, \frac{L_V(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})}{\Var(\by)} \;\in\; [0, 1].
    \label{eq:rspec-learner-defn}
\end{equation}
For closed linear $\Hcal_d$, $f^\star_{\Hcal_d} = \bP_{\Hcal_d}\by$ and
$\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by) = \|\bP_{\Hcal_d}\by\|^2/\|\by\|^2$, the classical
projection $R^2$.  For non-linear $\Hcal_d$ (gradient-boosted trees,
neural networks with weight constraints, $k$-NN with local averaging),
$\Rspec$ is the population-variance-explained ceiling of the class.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Examples of $\Hcal_d$ and $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)$]
\label{rem:examples}
\begin{itemize}[label=$\bullet$,leftmargin=*]
\item \emph{Categorical (one-hot) learner.}  $\Hcal_d = \{$constants on $T^c\}$
    (the learner sees one-hot indicators for $v \in T$ only, and is
    forced to a constant prediction on $T^c$).  Then
    $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by) = 0$ for centred $\by$.
\item \emph{Linear regressor on features $\bphi$.}
    $\Hcal_d = \{(\langle \bphi(v), \bm{\beta}\rangle)_{v \in V} : \bm{\beta} \in \R^d\}$;
    $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)$ is the projection-$R^2$ of $\by$ on
    $\mathrm{col}(\bphi)$.
\item \emph{Kernel ridge with kernel $\mathcal{K}$.}  $\Hcal_d$ is the
    column span of the kernel matrix.
\item \emph{Sufficiently expressive MLP / GBM.}  $\Hcal_d = \R^N$, so
    $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by) = 1$ and the bound becomes vacuous --- as
    expected, since an arbitrarily expressive learner has no structural
    floor.
\end{itemize}
\end{remark}

\subsection{Assumptions}

\begin{assumption}[Bounded centred target]
\label{a:target}
$\|\by\|_\infty \leq M$ and $\bone^\top \by = 0$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[ERM learner]
\label{a:erm}
The learner $\Acal$ is an empirical risk minimiser on $\Hcal_d$:
$\hat f_\Acal(T) \in \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d} L_T(f)$.  The hypothesis
class is truncated to $[-M, M]$ and admits a Rademacher complexity
$\mathcal{R}_n(\Hcal_d)$ in the sense of \citet[][\S 3.3]{Mohri2018book}.
\end{assumption}

\section{Main results}
\label{sec:main-results}

\begin{theorem}[Universal spectral lower bound]
\label{thm:universal-lower-bound}
Under Assumptions~\ref{a:target}--\ref{a:erm}, for any evaluation
protocol $\Pi$ satisfying $\E_T[L_T(f)] = L_V(f)$ for all deterministic
$f \in \R^N$ (in particular $\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$),
\begin{equation}
    \E_{T \sim \Dcal_\Pi}\!\big[L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))\big]
    \;\geq\; \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)\big) \cdot \Var(\by).
    \label{eq:universal-lower-bound}
\end{equation}
The bound is \emph{exact in expectation}: there is no concentration
slack term.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Universal upper bound, sharp transductive form]
\label{thm:universal-upper-bound}
Under Assumptions~\ref{a:target}--\ref{a:erm}, with probability $\geq 1
- \eta$ over $T \sim \Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$,
\begin{equation}
    L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))
    \;\leq\; \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)\big) \cdot \Var(\by)
        \;+\; 2 \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\ell \circ \Hcal_d)
        \;+\; 5.05\, M^2 \sqrt{\frac{(n + u)\log(2/\eta)}{n \cdot u}},
    \label{eq:universal-upper-bound}
\end{equation}
where $u := N - n$ is the held-out cardinality,
$\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\Hcal_d)$ is the transductive
Rademacher complexity of \citet[][\S 3]{ElYanivPechyony2006},
$\ell$ is the truncated square loss, and the constant
$5.05$ is from \citet[][Theorem~1]{ElYanivPechyony2006}.  For
learners with $\mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\Hcal_d) = O(\sqrt{d/u})$
(linear, kernel-ridge, MLP with bounded weights, gradient-boosted
trees of bounded depth), the leading-order slack is
$O(M \sqrt{d/u} + M^2/\sqrt{u}) = O(M^2/\sqrt{N - n})$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Berry--Esseen minimax lower bound on the slack]
\label{thm:minimax-lower-bound}
For any hypothesis class $\Hcal_d \subsetneq \R^N$, any $M > 0$, any
sample sizes $1 \leq n \leq N - 2$, and any confidence level $\eta \in
(0, 1/3)$, there exists a signal $\by^\star \in \Hcal_d^\perp$ with
$\|\by^\star\|_\infty = M$ such that
\begin{equation}
    \PR_T\!\Big[
        L_{T^c}(\hat f) - \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by^\star)\big)\Var(\by^\star)
        \;\geq\; c \cdot M^2 / \sqrt{N - n}
    \Big]
    \;\geq\; 1 - \eta
    \label{eq:minimax-lower-bound}
\end{equation}
for an absolute constant $c > 0$ (Berry--Esseen, $c \approx 1/16$
suffices), uniformly in $\hat f$.  No estimator --- including one with
oracle access to $\by^\star$ on $V$ --- can drive the slack of
\eqref{eq:universal-upper-bound} below
$\Omega(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$ in the worst case.
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Cram\'er--Rao efficiency for graph-supervised learning]
\label{cor:cramer-rao}
Combining Theorems~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}--\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound},
the minimax slack rate at protocol $\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ on signal class
$\Ycal_{\Hcal_d, M} := \{\by \in \R^N : \|\by\|_\infty \leq M, \bone^\top
\by = 0\}$ is
\begin{equation}
    \frR_n(\Hcal_d, M, \eta)
    \;=\; \Theta\!\left(\frac{M^2}{\sqrt{N - n}} + \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\Hcal_d)\right),
    \label{eq:cramer-rao-rate}
\end{equation}
with absolute constants on both sides.  Upper and lower bounds match
in rate ($1/\sqrt{N-n}$) and to a small multiplicative constant
(within a factor of $\approx 10$, modulo the Berry--Esseen constant
and the $5.05$ transductive Rademacher constant; tightening to a
factor of $\approx 4$ is possible via a multi-point Fano argument,
sketched in
\texttt{notes/04\_sharp\_transductive\_constant.tex}~\S 4).
The empirical-risk-minimising learner is therefore minimax-efficient
on graph-supervised regression problems under leave-node-out, in the
sense that the classical Cram\'er--Rao bound characterises
minimax-efficient estimators in regular parametric estimation.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Centring]
\label{rem:centring}
If $\by$ is not centred, replace $\by$ throughout by $\by - \bar y \bone$,
where $\bar y := N^{-1}\bone^\top \by$.  Equivalently, augment
$\Hcal_d$ to contain the constant vector $\bone$, which absorbs the
mean predictor.  All standard learners satisfy this implicitly through
a bias / intercept term.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Why the Pesenson sampling quality $\sigma_T$ does not appear]
\label{rem:no-sigma-T-needed}
An earlier formulation of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}
(working notes \texttt{notes/01\_universal\_bound\_proof.tex} in the
companion repository, withdrawn in \texttt{notes/03\_non\_realisable\_resolution.tex})
expressed the slack as $O(\rho(\Pi) /
\sqrt{n \cdot \sigma_\Pi(\Hcal_d)})$, where $\sigma_\Pi(\Hcal_d) :=
\E_T[\lambda_{\min}(\bB^\top \bP_T \bB)]$ is the
Pesenson--Anis--Gadde--Ortega sampling quality of $\Pi$ for $\Hcal_d$.
This formulation was an artefact of using the Tikhonov-regularised
Pesenson-ridge estimator as the witness predictor.  In fact, the
population-risk minimiser $f^\star_{\Hcal_d}$ itself serves as a
witness without invoking the sampling-restricted inverse $(\bB^\top
\bP_T \bB)^{-1}$, eliminating the $\sigma_T$ factor entirely.  The
proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} below uses this
cleaner witness, mirroring the proof of Theorem~1(c) of
\citet{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound}.  The
graph-Fisher-information / inverse-problem theory of
\citet{BauerPereverzev2005,Cavalier2008} --- relevant for noisy
continuous Hilbert-space inverse problems --- does not apply to the
finite-population empirical-risk problem treated here.
\end{remark}

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}}
\label{sec:lower-bound-proof}

The proof has three short steps.

\paragraph{Step 1 — Functional floor on $L_V$ (Bessel-projection inequality).}

\begin{lemma}[Bessel projection floor]
\label{lem:bessel}
For any $\hat f \in \Hcal_d$,
$N \cdot L_V(\hat f) = \|\hat f - \by\|^2 \;\geq\; \|f^\star_{\Hcal_d} - \by\|^2 = N \cdot (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$f^\star_{\Hcal_d}$ is the population-risk minimiser by definition.
Hence $L_V(\hat f) \geq L_V(f^\star_{\Hcal_d}) = (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d,
\by))\Var(\by)$ for every $\hat f \in \Hcal_d$.  By Assumption~\ref{a:erm},
$\hat f_\Acal(T) \in \Hcal_d$, so the inequality applies pointwise in $T$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Step 2 — Transduction identity.}

\begin{lemma}[Population--train--holdout decomposition]
\label{lem:transduction}
For any deterministic $f \in \R^N$ and any $T \subset V$ with $|T| = n$,
\begin{equation}
    L_V(f) \;=\; \frac{n}{N}\, L_T(f) \;+\; \frac{N-n}{N}\, L_{T^c}(f),
    \label{eq:transduction-identity}
\end{equation}
equivalently $L_{T^c}(f) = \rho(\Pi) L_V(f) - (\rho(\Pi) - 1) L_T(f)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$N \cdot L_V(f) = \sum_{v \in V}(f(v) - y_v)^2 = \sum_{v \in T}(\ldots)
+ \sum_{v \in T^c}(\ldots) = n \cdot L_T(f) + (N-n) \cdot L_{T^c}(f)$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Step 3 — Combination.}

By Lemma~\ref{lem:transduction} applied to $f = \hat f_\Acal(T)$ and
taken in expectation over $T$,
\begin{equation}
    \E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))]
    \;=\; \rho(\Pi) \cdot \E_T[L_V(\hat f_\Acal(T))]
        \;-\; (\rho(\Pi) - 1) \cdot \E_T[L_T(\hat f_\Acal(T))].
    \label{eq:lower-step3-expectation}
\end{equation}
By Lemma~\ref{lem:bessel}, $L_V(\hat f_\Acal(T)) \geq (1 -
\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)$ pointwise in $T$, hence
$\E_T[L_V(\hat f_\Acal(T))] \geq (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)$.

For the $L_T$ term, by Assumption~\ref{a:erm} (ERM property) and
$f^\star_{\Hcal_d} \in \Hcal_d$,
$L_T(\hat f_\Acal(T)) \leq L_T(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})$ pointwise in $T$.
Taking expectation and using the protocol hypothesis $\E_T[L_T(f)] = L_V(f)$
for deterministic $f$,
\begin{equation}
    \E_T[L_T(\hat f_\Acal(T))]
    \;\leq\; \E_T[L_T(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})]
    \;=\; L_V(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})
    \;=\; (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)) \Var(\by).
    \label{eq:lower-step3-LT}
\end{equation}
Setting $A := (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)) \Var(\by)$ and substituting
into \eqref{eq:lower-step3-expectation},
$$
\E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))] \;\geq\; \rho(\Pi) \cdot A - (\rho(\Pi) - 1) \cdot A \;=\; A.
$$
This is Eq.~\eqref{eq:universal-lower-bound}.\qed

\begin{remark}[Why the bound is exact in expectation]
\label{rem:exact-in-expectation}
The two slack terms (from upper-bounding $L_V$ above by the Bessel
floor, and from upper-bounding $L_T$ below by the ERM-on-projection
inequality) cancel exactly through the transduction identity.  This
mirrors the structural unbiasedness condition under which the classical
Cram\'er--Rao bound holds with equality.  A high-probability version
of the bound carries a $O(\rho M^2/\sqrt{n})$ slack from the
Hoeffding--Serfling concentration of \eqref{eq:lower-step3-LT}; see
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} for the matching upper bound.
\end{remark}

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}}
\label{sec:upper-bound-proof}

The proof structure mirrors that of part~(c) of Theorem~1 of
\citet[][\S 5.2]{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound}, with the
population-risk minimiser $f^\star_{\Hcal_d}$ replacing the specific
compositional projector $\bP_{\Scal_{\mathrm{comp}}}\by$ used there.

\paragraph{Step 1 — In-sample concentration of the witness.}

The population-risk minimiser $f^\star_{\Hcal_d} \in \Hcal_d$ is fixed
(not a function of $T$).  By Serfling's without-replacement Hoeffding
inequality \citep{Serfling1974,BardenetMaillard2015} applied to the
bounded sequence $\{(f^\star_{\Hcal_d}(v) - y_v)^2\}_{v \in V}$ ---
which has population mean $L_V(f^\star_{\Hcal_d}) = (1 -
\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)$ and supremum at most $4 M^2$ --- with
probability $\geq 1 - \eta/2$ over $T \sim \Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$,
\begin{equation}
    L_T(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})
    \;\leq\; (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)) \Var(\by)
        + 4 M^2 \sqrt{\frac{(1 - (n-1)/N) \log(2/\eta)}{2 n}}.
    \label{eq:upper-step1-serfling}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 2 — ERM property.}

By Assumption~\ref{a:erm}, $L_T(\hat f_\Acal(T)) \leq L_T(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})$
pointwise in $T$, since $f^\star_{\Hcal_d} \in \Hcal_d$ and ERM
minimises $L_T$ over $\Hcal_d$.

\paragraph{Step 3 — Rademacher generalisation.}

The \emph{transductive} Rademacher bound of
\citet[][Theorem~1]{ElYanivPechyony2006} (rather than the inductive
Mohri Theorem~3.7 \citealp{Mohri2018book}, which would route the
argument through population risk $L_V$ and inflate the slack by a
factor of $\sqrt{\rho(\Pi) - 1}$), combined with the Talagrand
contraction lemma \citep{Talagrand1996concentration} ($2M$-Lipschitz
square loss), gives with probability $\geq 1 - \eta/2$:
\begin{equation}
    L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))
    \;\leq\; L_T^{\pi}(\hat f_\Acal(T))
        + 2 \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\ell \circ \Hcal_d)
        + 5.05\, M^2 \sqrt{\frac{(n + u) \log(2/\eta)}{n u}}.
    \label{eq:upper-step3-trans-rademacher}
\end{equation}
Crucially, the transductive bound \emph{directly} controls $L_{T^c} -
L_T^\pi$, avoiding the intermediate population-risk inflation by
$\rho(\Pi)$ that the inductive Mohri bound would incur.

\paragraph{Step 4 — Conclusion.}

By Assumption~\ref{a:erm} (ERM property), $L_T^\pi(\hat f_\Acal(T))
\leq L_T^\pi(f^\star_{\Hcal_d})$.  Combining with
\eqref{eq:upper-step1-serfling} and \eqref{eq:upper-step3-trans-rademacher},
$$
L_{T^c}(\hat f_\Acal(T))
\;\leq\; (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by))\Var(\by)
    + 2 \mathcal{R}^{\mathrm{trans}}_n(\ell \circ \Hcal_d)
    + 5.05\, M^2 \sqrt{(n+u)\log(2/\eta)/(nu)},
$$
yielding \eqref{eq:universal-upper-bound}.  See
\texttt{notes/04\_sharp\_transductive\_constant.tex} for the
derivation of the factor-$\sqrt{\rho-1}$ improvement over an
intermediate version of this theorem that routed through inductive
Mohri.\qed

\section{Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound}}
\label{sec:minimax-proof}

We construct an adversarial signal whose Berry--Esseen anticoncentration
on the held-out residual already forces an $\Omega(M^2/\sqrt{N-n})$
deviation between $L_{T^c}(\hat f)$ and its population value,
irrespective of estimator choice.

\paragraph{Construction of $\by^\star$.}

Pick any unit-infty vector $\bu \in \Hcal_d^\perp$ (exists because
$\Hcal_d \subsetneq \R^N$).  Rescale so $\bu$ has $N/2$ entries equal
to $\pm 1$ (in any sign pattern respecting $\bone^\top \bu = 0$) and
zero entries elsewhere.  Set $\by^\star := M \bu$.  Then $\by^\star
\in \Hcal_d^\perp$, $\|\by^\star\|_\infty = M$, $\bone^\top \by^\star
= 0$.  Since $\by^\star \in \Hcal_d^\perp$,
$\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by^\star) = 0$ and the lower bound reference value is
$\Var(\by^\star) = M^2/2$.

\paragraph{Oracle slack.}

Any predictor $\hat f \in \Hcal_d$ (oracle or not) achieves population
loss $L_V(\hat f) \geq L_V(0) = \Var(\by^\star) = M^2/2$, since
$\by^\star \in \Hcal_d^\perp$ means the best in-class predictor is the
zero function.  Define $\Delta_T := L_{T^c}(0) - \Var(\by^\star)$.
For $\hat f = 0$,
$$
L_{T^c}(0) - \Var(\by^\star)
= \frac{1}{|T^c|}\sum_{v \in T^c} (y^\star_v)^2 - \frac{1}{N}\sum_v (y^\star_v)^2.
$$
Define $X_v := (y^\star_v)^2 - \Var(\by^\star) = M^2 \mathbf{1}_{\{v
\in \mathrm{supp}(\bu)\}} - M^2/2$.  The $X_v$ are centred ($\sum_v X_v
= 0$), bounded in $[-M^2/2, M^2/2]$, and have population variance
$N^{-1}\sum X_v^2 = M^4/4$.

\paragraph{Berry--Esseen on sampling without replacement.}

By the Berry--Esseen theorem for sampling without replacement
\citep{Bikelis1969,Bentkus2003}, the standardised sum
$\frac{1}{\sqrt{|T^c| M^4/4}}\sum_{v \in T^c} X_v$ converges to
$\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ in Kolmogorov distance at rate $O(M^2/\sqrt{(N-n)
M^4/4}) = O(1/\sqrt{N-n})$.  For any threshold $\kappa$, the deviation
event
$$
\PR_T\!\left[\Delta_T \geq \kappa \cdot \frac{M^2}{2\sqrt{N-n}}\right]
$$
is, up to a Berry--Esseen correction of order $1/\sqrt{N-n}$, equal to
$\Phi(-\kappa)$.

\paragraph{Combine.}

For $\eta < 1/3$, choose $\kappa$ such that $\Phi(-\kappa) = 1 - \eta - 14/\sqrt{N-n}$
(absorbing the Berry--Esseen constant into the displayed constant).
For $N - n \geq 100$ and $\eta < 1/3$, $\kappa \geq 1$ suffices, giving
$$
\PR_T\!\left[\Delta_T \geq \frac{M^2}{2\sqrt{N-n}}\right] \;\geq\; 1 - \eta.
$$
Since the oracle predictor $\hat f = 0$ \emph{dominates} every other
$\hat f \in \Hcal_d$ on $\by^\star \in \Hcal_d^\perp$, this lower bound
applies to every learner, yielding \eqref{eq:minimax-lower-bound} with
$c = 1/2$ (resp.\ $c = 1/16$ uniformly across smaller $N - n$).\qed

\section{Corollaries}
\label{sec:corollaries}

\subsection{C1 — Encoding-gap bound for materials informatics}
\label{subsec:c1}

Take two learners on the same graph $G$ with hypothesis classes
$\Hcal_{\mathrm{cat}} := \{f : f \text{ constant on } T^c \text{ for
every } T\}$ (the one-hot categorical learner of MLST Theorem~1) and
$\Hcal_{\mathrm{comp}}$ a closed linear subspace of $\R^N$ (the
compositional feature subspace).  Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}
applied to each gives
\begin{align*}
\E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{cat}})]   &\geq \Var(\by), \\
\E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{comp}})] &\geq (1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_{\mathrm{comp}}, \by))\Var(\by).
\end{align*}
Subtracting, the structural gap is
$$
\E_T[L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{cat}}) - L_{T^c}(\hat f_{\mathrm{comp}})]
\geq \Rspec(\Hcal_{\mathrm{comp}}, \by) \cdot \Var(\by),
$$
recovering Theorem~1(d) of \citet{FossePallares2026applicabilityBound} exactly.
Combining with Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} on the
compositional learner gives a high-probability version with the slack
of \eqref{eq:universal-upper-bound}.  The empirical validation on the
8-task MatBench panel and the cross-domain bike-share and MovieLens
controls is reported in detail in the companion paper.

\subsection{C2 --- Negative transfer (spectral disjointness)}
\label{subsec:c2}

We instantiate the universal framework on the negative-transfer
problem: a learner trained on a \emph{source} task is evaluated on a
\emph{target} task, and we ask under what structural conditions
transfer must fail.  The literature on negative transfer
\citep{Rosenstein2005,WangDaiPoczos2019,Zhang2022survey} formalises
the phenomenon empirically but has not, to our knowledge, provided a
model-free lower bound expressed in the spectral language of the
data-task graph.  C2 fills this gap as a one-page corollary of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}.

\paragraph{Setup.}  Let $G = (V, E)$ be the joint similarity graph on
the union $V = V_{\mathrm{src}} \cup V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ (disjoint
union), with $|V_{\mathrm{src}}| = n_s$, $|V_{\mathrm{tgt}}| = n_t$,
$N := n_s + n_t$.  The graph encodes the chosen task-relatedness
geometry: examples that are jointly close in feature space are
adjacent, whether they belong to the source task, the target task, or
across.  Let $L = \bU \boldsymbol{\Lambda} \bU^\top$ be the
symmetric-normalised Laplacian of $G$ with eigenvectors $\bU = [\bu_1,
\ldots, \bu_N]$.  The joint signal $\by \in \R^N$ is the concatenation
of the source labels $\by_{\mathrm{src}} \in \R^{n_s}$ and the target
labels $\by_{\mathrm{tgt}} \in \R^{n_t}$; each is centred and bounded
($\|\by_{\mathrm{src}}\|_\infty \leq M$,
$\|\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|_\infty \leq M$).

The \emph{leave-task-out protocol} $\Pi_{\mathrm{LTO}}$ is the
\emph{deterministic} split $T = V_{\mathrm{src}}$, $T^c =
V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$.  A \emph{transfer learner} $\Acal$ is an ERM on the
source training set: $\hat f_\Acal := \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d}
L_{V_{\mathrm{src}}}(f)$ where $\Hcal_d$ is the learner's hypothesis
class on $\R^N$ (a linear head on shared features, a foundation-model
embedding's output space, etc.).  The evaluation metric is the
target loss $L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(\hat f_\Acal)$.

\begin{definition}[Negative transfer magnitude]
\label{def:nt-magnitude}
The \emph{negative-transfer magnitude} of the transfer learner $\Acal$
on the source--target pair $(\by_{\mathrm{src}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}})$
is the excess target loss over the in-class optimum on the target:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{NT}(\Acal; \by_{\mathrm{src}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}})
    \;:=\; L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(\hat f_\Acal) - L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(f^\star_{\Hcal_d, \mathrm{tgt}}),
    \label{eq:nt-magnitude}
\end{equation}
where $f^\star_{\Hcal_d, \mathrm{tgt}} := \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d}
L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(f)$ is the target-only ideal predictor in $\Hcal_d$
(unreachable by a source-only learner unless the source and target
populations agree on the projection structure of $\by$).
$\mathrm{NT}(\Acal) > 0$ certifies that transferring source-trained
information \emph{harms} target performance relative to the best
in-class target-only baseline; $\mathrm{NT}(\Acal) = 0$ certifies that
the source-only learner already achieves the in-class target optimum.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Spectral disjointness of source and target signals]
\label{def:spectral-disjointness}
Let $\hat y_{\mathrm{src},k} := \bu_k^\top \tilde\by_{\mathrm{src}}$
and $\hat y_{\mathrm{tgt},k} := \bu_k^\top \tilde\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}$
be the graph Fourier coefficients of the zero-padded source and target
signals $\tilde\by_{\mathrm{src}}, \tilde\by_{\mathrm{tgt}} \in \R^N$
(where the signal is set to zero on the complementary node set).  The
pair $(\by_{\mathrm{src}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}})$ is
\emph{$K$-spectrally disjoint with respect to $\Hcal_d$} if there
exists an index set $\mathcal{I} \subseteq \{1, \ldots, N\}$ with
$|\mathcal{I}| \leq K$ and a partition $\mathcal{I} = \mathcal{I}_s
\sqcup \mathcal{I}_t$ such that
\begin{equation}
    \hat y_{\mathrm{src}, k} = 0 \;\;\forall\, k \in \mathcal{I}_t,
    \qquad
    \hat y_{\mathrm{tgt}, k} = 0 \;\;\forall\, k \in \mathcal{I}_s,
    \qquad
    \Hcal_d \subseteq \mathrm{span}(\{\bu_k\}_{k \in \mathcal{I}}).
    \label{eq:spectral-disjointness}
\end{equation}
That is: the learner's reachable subspace lies inside a low-frequency
band $\mathrm{span}(\{\bu_k\}_{k \in \mathcal{I}})$, and within that
band the source signal is supported on $\mathcal{I}_s$ while the
target signal is supported on the complementary $\mathcal{I}_t$.
\end{definition}

\begin{remark}[Geometric interpretation]
\label{rem:spectral-disjointness-geometry}
Spectral disjointness expresses, in the joint Laplacian eigenbasis, the
condition that source and target labels carry information about
\emph{different} frequency modes of the data-task graph.  In a
materials-informatics context, this corresponds to the source and
target properties being driven by structurally orthogonal compositional
trends.  In a urban-mobility context, it corresponds to source-city
mode-share variations and target-city mode-share variations being
correlated with non-overlapping commune-cluster eigenmodes.  In every
case, the practical reading is: \emph{source data carries no
in-class-projectable information about the target signal}.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Universal negative-transfer lower bound]
\label{thm:nt-lower-bound}
Let $\Hcal_d \subseteq \R^N$ be a closed linear subspace of dimension
$d$ ($\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \cdot)$ is then the projection $R^2$).  Suppose
the source--target pair $(\by_{\mathrm{src}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}})$ is
$K$-spectrally disjoint with respect to $\Hcal_d$
(Definition~\ref{def:spectral-disjointness}).  Then for any ERM
transfer learner $\Acal$ with hypothesis class $\Hcal_d$,
\begin{equation}
    \E_\Acal\!\big[\mathrm{NT}(\Acal; \by_{\mathrm{src}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}})\big]
    \;\geq\; \frac{1}{n_t}\Big(\|\bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{src}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
        + \|\bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}\Big),
    \label{eq:nt-lower-bound}
\end{equation}
where $\|\cdot\|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$ is the $\ell_2$ norm restricted to
$V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$.  In particular, $\mathrm{NT}(\Acal) \geq
\Rspec(\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}) \cdot
\Var_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(\by_{\mathrm{tgt}})$ whenever
$\bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{tgt}} \neq 0$.  Negative transfer is
unavoidable.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
By Assumption~\ref{a:erm} (ERM property) applied to the source
training, the source-trained learner converges, in expectation over
the algorithm's internal randomness, to the source-population-risk
minimiser:
$\E_\Acal[\hat f_\Acal] = \bP_{\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{src}}}}
\tilde\by_{\mathrm{src}}$ on $V_{\mathrm{src}}$ and the corresponding
linear extension to $V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ via the basis representation of
$\Hcal_d$.  Equivalently, in the closed-linear-subspace case,
$\E_\Acal[\hat f_\Acal] = \bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{src}}$ when
$V_{\mathrm{src}}$ is a uniqueness set for $\Hcal_d$ (which we assume,
ensuring identifiability of the source ERM).

Let $f_s := \bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{src}}$ and $f_t :=
\bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}$.  By spectral disjointness,
$f_s$ and $f_t$ live on disjoint Fourier supports
$\mathcal{I}_s, \mathcal{I}_t \subseteq \mathcal{I}$ in the joint
$\bU$-basis, so $\langle f_s, f_t \rangle = 0$ (orthogonal in $\R^N$,
hence also in their restriction to $V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ up to a controlled
correction; see Remark~\ref{rem:disjointness-restriction-control}
below).

Compute the target excess loss:
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{NT}(\Acal)
    &= L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(\hat f_\Acal) - L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(f^\star_{\Hcal_d, \mathrm{tgt}}).
\end{align*}
The minimum of $L_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(f)$ over $\Hcal_d$ is attained at
$f^\star_{\Hcal_d, \mathrm{tgt}} = \bP_{\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}}
\tilde\by_{\mathrm{tgt}} = f_t|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$ (modulo the same
restriction correction, controlled by the sampling quality of
$V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ for $\Hcal_d$; in the spectral-disjoint regime this
correction is zero to leading order, as $f_t$ already lies in
$\Hcal_d$ by construction).  Hence
\begin{align*}
    \mathrm{NT}(\Acal)
    &= \frac{1}{n_t}\|\hat f_\Acal - \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
       - \frac{1}{n_t}\|f_t - \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}\\
    &= \frac{1}{n_t}\Big[\|\hat f_\Acal\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
         - 2\langle \hat f_\Acal, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
         - \|f_t\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
         + 2\langle f_t, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}\Big].
\end{align*}
Taking $\E_\Acal[\hat f_\Acal] = f_s$ and using
$\langle f_s, \by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} = \langle f_s, f_t \rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$
(both $f_s$ and $f_t$ are in $\Hcal_d$, $\by_{\mathrm{tgt}} - f_t \in
\Hcal_d^\perp|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$),
\begin{align*}
    \E_\Acal[\mathrm{NT}(\Acal)]
    &= \frac{1}{n_t}\Big[\|f_s\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} - 2\langle f_s, f_t\rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} + \|f_t\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}\Big]\\
    &= \frac{1}{n_t}\|f_s - f_t\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}.
\end{align*}
By spectral disjointness, $\langle f_s, f_t \rangle = 0$ in $\R^N$
(both have disjoint Fourier supports), and by the controlled
restriction $\langle f_s, f_t \rangle_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} = O(\text{sampling
correction})$ which we absorb into the bound at leading order, hence
\begin{equation*}
    \E_\Acal[\mathrm{NT}(\Acal)]
    \;\geq\; \frac{1}{n_t}\Big(\|f_s\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} + \|f_t\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}\Big)
    \;=\; \frac{1}{n_t}\Big(\|\bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{src}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} + \|\bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}\|^2_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}\Big),
\end{equation*}
which is \eqref{eq:nt-lower-bound}.  If $\bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}
\neq 0$ then by Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} applied to
$\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$, $\Rspec(\Hcal_d|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}},
\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}) > 0$ and the lower bound is strictly positive,
yielding \emph{unavoidable} negative transfer.\qed
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Sampling-restriction correction]
\label{rem:disjointness-restriction-control}
The proof above assumes that the inner product $\langle f_s, f_t
\rangle$ on $V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ equals the global inner product to
leading order.  Formally, this requires that $V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ is a
\emph{near-uniqueness} set for $\Hcal_d$ in the sense
that $\sigma_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}(\Hcal_d)$
(Section~\ref{sec:setup}) is bounded away from zero.  Under this
condition --- which is satisfied whenever $n_t \geq c \cdot d$ for a
suitable constant $c$ depending on the leverage scores of $\Hcal_d$ on
$V_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ --- the restriction correction is $O(1/\sqrt{n_t})$
and does not affect the leading-order bound \eqref{eq:nt-lower-bound}.
A non-uniqueness set ($\sigma_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}} = 0$) renders the
target ERM ill-posed; this is the genuine pathology, separate from the
negative-transfer phenomenon proper.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[NT vacuity criterion: spectral alignment]
\label{cor:nt-vacuity}
Conversely, if the source and target signals are \emph{spectrally
aligned} on $\Hcal_d$ --- meaning $\bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{src}}|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}
= \bP_{\Hcal_d}\tilde\by_{\mathrm{tgt}}|_{V_{\mathrm{tgt}}}$ --- then
the source-trained ERM achieves the target in-class optimum:
$\E_\Acal[\mathrm{NT}(\Acal)] = 0$.  Transfer is then \emph{exactly}
as effective as target-only training (with the slack of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} carrying over verbatim, replacing
$n$ by $n_s + n_t$ for the joint training pool).
\end{corollary}

This dichotomy --- vacuous transfer under spectral alignment,
unavoidable harm under spectral disjointness --- localises the
negative-transfer phenomenon to a precise spectral-geometric property
of the (joint-graph, source-signal, target-signal) triple, independent
of the regressor's architecture or training procedure.

\paragraph{Empirical validation programme.}  The corollary is amenable
to empirical falsification in any setting where two related supervised
tasks share a graph: paired MatBench tasks (e.g.,
\texttt{mp\_e\_form} $\to$ \texttt{mp\_gap}) on the composition graph,
QM9-derived molecular properties on shared structure graphs, or
mode-choice models trained in one French commune cluster and evaluated
on another in the \texttt{mobility-applicability-bound} panel.
A dedicated empirical pre-registration is in preparation as the
companion repository \texttt{negative-transfer-corollary} of the
\texttt{cycling-data-lab} organisation.

\subsection{C3 --- Active learning (label complexity dual)}
\label{subsec:c3}

We instantiate the universal framework on the active-learning problem:
the learner is allowed to \emph{choose} the training set $T \subseteq V$
adaptively, and we ask how this choice affects the slack of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}.  The literature on
active-learning label complexity
\citep{Settles2009,Hanneke2014,BeygelzimerDasguptaLangford2009,DasguptaHsu2008}
gives essentially-tight $O(d \log(1/\epsilon))$ bounds for finite-VC
classes; the spectral-graph subset
\citep{GuilloryBilmes2009,GaddeAnisOrtega2014,DrineasMahoney2018,Avron2017,Puy2018}
gives leverage-score-based bounds that depend on the operator-spectral
geometry of $\Hcal_d$.  C3 connects the two by deriving the
leverage-score sampling rate from the upper bound of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} via importance weighting.

\paragraph{Setup.}  Replace $\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ (uniform sampling) by
a general protocol $\Pi_\pi$ in which each $v \in V$ is included in $T$
independently with probability $\pi(v)$, normalised so $\sum_v \pi(v)
= n$.  The training loss is replaced by its
\emph{importance-weighted} variant:
\begin{equation}
    L_T^\pi(\hat f) \;:=\; \frac{1}{N} \sum_{v \in T} \frac{(\hat f(v) - y_v)^2}{\pi(v)},
    \label{eq:weighted-loss}
\end{equation}
which is an unbiased estimator of $L_V(\hat f)$ under
$\E_T[\,\mathbf{1}_{\{v \in T\}}\,] = \pi(v)$.

\begin{lemma}[Importance-weighted transduction identity]
\label{lem:weighted-transduction}
For any deterministic $f \in \R^N$ and any $\Pi_\pi$ with $\E_T[L_T^\pi(f)] = L_V(f)$,
\begin{equation}
    \E_T[L_{T^c}(f)] \;=\; L_V(f) \;+\; \tfrac{n}{N - n}\,\big(L_V(f) - \E_T[L_T^\pi(f)]\big)
    \;=\; L_V(f),
    \label{eq:weighted-transduction}
\end{equation}
the last equality holding because $\E_T[L_T^\pi(f)] = L_V(f)$ exactly
under the unbiased weighting.  Equivalently:
$\E_T[L_{T^c}(f)] = L_V(f)$ for any unbiasedly weighted training set,
recovering the uniform-sampling exchangeability of
Lemma~\ref{lem:transduction} as the special case $\pi \equiv n/N$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$\E_T[\mathbf{1}_{\{v \in T\}}/\pi(v)] = 1$ for every $v$, so
$\E_T[L_T^\pi(f)] = N^{-1}\sum_v (f(v) - y_v)^2 = L_V(f)$.  By
linearity of expectation and the partition $V = T \sqcup T^c$,
$\E_T[L_{T^c}(f)] = L_V(f) - \E_T[L_T(f) \cdot |T|/N]$.  Under uniform
sampling without replacement, $|T| = n$ deterministically and the
identity reduces to Lemma~\ref{lem:transduction}; under Bernoulli
sampling with $\sum \pi = n$, the same identity holds in expectation
over $|T|$.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Active-learning slack.}  The proof of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} carries through with $L_T$
replaced by $L_T^\pi$, except in the Serfling without-replacement
Hoeffding step, which acquires an importance-weighting factor.

\begin{theorem}[Active-learning upper bound]
\label{thm:al-upper-bound}
Under Assumptions~\ref{a:target}--\ref{a:erm}, replace
$\Pi_{\mathrm{LSO}}$ by $\Pi_\pi$ (Bernoulli sampling with
$\sum_v \pi(v) = n$) and the ERM by importance-weighted ERM,
$\hat f^\pi_\Acal(T) := \arg\min_{f \in \Hcal_d} L_T^\pi(f)$.  With
probability $\geq 1 - \eta$ over $T \sim \Pi_\pi$,
\begin{equation}
    L_{T^c}(\hat f^\pi_\Acal(T))
    \;\leq\; \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)\big) \cdot \Var(\by)
        \;+\; 2 \mathcal{R}_n(\Hcal_d)
        \;+\; \frac{4 M^2}{\sqrt{2n}}\sqrt{\E_v\!\big[\pi(v)^{-1}\big]\log\!\tfrac{2}{\eta}}.
    \label{eq:al-upper-bound}
\end{equation}
The slack is minimised over $\pi$ subject to $\sum \pi = n$ by the
\emph{leverage-score distribution}:
\begin{equation}
    \pi^\star(v) \;\propto\; \|\bB(v, :)\|^2,
    \qquad \text{normalised so } \sum_v \pi^\star(v) = n,
    \label{eq:leverage-score-defn}
\end{equation}
where $\bB \in \R^{N \times d}$ is an orthonormal basis of $\Hcal_d$.
Under $\pi = \pi^\star$, $\E_v[\pi^\star(v)^{-1}] = d \cdot N / n$ and
the slack becomes
\begin{equation}
    L_{T^c}(\hat f^{\pi^\star}_\Acal(T))
    \;\leq\; \big(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)\big) \cdot \Var(\by)
        \;+\; 2 \mathcal{R}_n(\Hcal_d)
        \;+\; \frac{4 M^2 \sqrt{d N \log(2/\eta)}}{n}.
    \label{eq:al-leverage-slack}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The witness-predictor argument of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} applies verbatim with
$f^\star_{\Hcal_d}$ as witness, since $f^\star_{\Hcal_d}$ does not
depend on $T$.  Hoeffding's inequality applied to the
importance-weighted bounded sequence $\{(f^\star_{\Hcal_d}(v) -
y_v)^2/\pi(v)\}_{v \in T}$ gives the slack term
$O(M^2 \sqrt{\E_v[\pi(v)^{-1}] / n})$ in place of the uniform
Hoeffding--Serfling slack $O(M^2/\sqrt{n})$.  The
importance-weighted transduction identity
(Lemma~\ref{lem:weighted-transduction}) eliminates the $\rho(\Pi)$
factor of \eqref{eq:universal-upper-bound}: under the unbiased
weighting, $\E_T[L_T^\pi(f)] = L_V(f)$ pointwise, so the conversion
from $L_T^\pi$ to $L_{T^c}$ no longer pays the $N/(N-n)$ inflation.

Minimisation of $\E_v[\pi(v)^{-1}]$ over $\pi$ subject to
$\sum \pi = n$ and the data-adapted normalisation $\sum_v \|\bB(v,
:)\|^2 = \tr(\bI_d) = d$ is a classical leverage-score problem
\citep[][Eq.~(3.3)]{DrineasMahoney2018} solved by
$\pi^\star(v) \propto \|\bB(v, :)\|^2$, yielding
$\E_v[\pi^\star(v)^{-1}] = N \cdot \sum_v \pi^\star(v) / n =
N \cdot d / n$ (by Cauchy--Schwarz).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Active learning is rate-optimal]
\label{cor:al-rate-optimal}
Under $\pi = \pi^\star$ (leverage-score sampling), the slack of
\eqref{eq:al-leverage-slack} is $O(M^2 \sqrt{d N \log(1/\eta)} / n)$,
i.e., the label complexity to reach excess risk $\epsilon$ is
\begin{equation}
    n_\epsilon(\Hcal_d, \by, \epsilon) \;=\; O\!\left(\frac{M^2 \sqrt{d N}}{\epsilon}\right),
    \label{eq:al-label-complexity}
\end{equation}
\emph{linear} in $1/\epsilon$ rather than the $1/\epsilon^2$ of
passive (uniform-sampling) learning.  This is the rate-optimal
$O(d/\epsilon)$ active-learning rate of
\citet{BalcanBeygelzimerLangford2006,DasguptaHsu2008,Hanneke2014}
specialised to the graph-supervised setting, with the leverage-score
distribution providing an explicit constructive sampling strategy.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[Matching minimax lower bound]
\label{rem:al-minimax-match}
A matching $\Omega(\sqrt{d N} / n)$ minimax lower bound under
leverage-score sampling follows by adapting
Theorem~\ref{thm:minimax-lower-bound} (Berry--Esseen on the
restricted-sample residual) to the importance-weighted regime.  The
adversarial signal $\by^\star \in \Hcal_d^\perp$ of the proof there
remains admissible; the only change is replacing the without-replacement
sum-variance $M^4/4$ by its importance-weighted analog
$M^4 \E_v[\pi^\star(v)^{-1}]/4 = M^4 dN/(4n)$, yielding a deviation
of $\Theta(M^2 \sqrt{d N}/n)$.  Hence the active-learning slack of
\eqref{eq:al-leverage-slack} is minimax-tight up to constants.
\end{remark}

The dichotomy --- passive learning at rate $1/\sqrt{n}$, active
learning at rate $1/n$ --- is the standard exponential improvement
in label complexity from active-learning theory, here re-derived as
a direct consequence of the universal framework and given an
explicit constructive sampler ($\pi^\star$, the leverage-score
distribution) determined entirely by the spectral geometry of
$\Hcal_d$.

\paragraph{Empirical validation programme.}  The leverage-score
active-learning protocol $\Pi_{\pi^\star}$ admits a direct empirical
falsification on any graph with a known feature subspace: compute
$\pi^\star(v) = \|\bB(v, :)\|^2$ from the Magpie basis on MatBench,
or from the IMD-4 basis on the French commune panel, then compare
the leave-node-out error under uniform vs leverage-score sampling.
The predicted improvement is $\sqrt{n/d}$ at fixed label budget.
A dedicated empirical pre-registration is in preparation as the
companion repository \texttt{active-learning-corollary} of the
\texttt{cycling-data-lab} organisation.

The cross-domain pilots provide a methodological check that the

\paragraph{The negative-$\Delta R^2$ regime is recoverable.}  The

eigenmodes of the rating matrix; the LSO gap is correctly negative.

pattern ($\Delta R^2 = -0.112$ with $\Rspec = 0.20$), again
consistent with the bound.

\paragraph{Encoder discrimination on shared substrate.}  On the same
8 MatBench tasks, the framework discriminates between encoders by
their $\Rspec$ value.  Replacing the Magpie compositional encoder by
the CHGNet foundation-model crystal embedding
\citep{Deng2023chgnet} on \texttt{matbench\_mp\_gap} reduces the
tail energy by $\Delta \epsilon_K = -0.13$ ($24$ pp; see d24 of
\texttt{materials-applicability-bound}), translating directly to a
larger $\Rspec$ and a tighter lower-bound floor.  This is the
predicted behaviour: a richer feature space increases $\Rspec(\Hcal_d,
\by)$ and tightens the bound on the held-out loss.

\paragraph{Replication in unrelated domains.}  The QSAR pilot
(molecular activity, $\Delta R^2 = 0.612$) and the AFLOW pilots
(bulk and shear moduli, $\Delta R^2 \approx 0.84$) replicate the
qualitative pattern on supervised regression problems with entirely
different physical content (chemoinformatics, ab-initio
solid-state).  Quantitative $\Rspec$ values for these pilots have
not been computed (they require constructing a domain-appropriate
similarity graph, which is non-trivial for QSAR molecules and
which we have not pre-registered); however, the consistent direction
and magnitude of $\Delta R^2$ in domains where $\Rspec$ is
\emph{expected} to be high provides directional confirmation.

\section{Discussion and open questions}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the bound says, and what it does not}

Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound} establishes that the expected
leave-node-out error of any learner is bounded below by the irreducible
component $(1 - \Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)) \Var(\by)$.  This quantity depends
only on three things: the population variance of the target, the
hypothesis class of the learner, and the projection of the target onto
that class.  It does \emph{not} depend on the regressor's training
algorithm, the choice of optimiser, the random seed, or the
hyperparameters --- all of which can only affect how closely the slack
of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound} is achieved.

Equivalently, the bound says: \emph{there is no algorithmic trick to
beat the structural floor}.  If your hypothesis class explains $40\%$
of the population variance, no choice of optimiser, regulariser, or
ensembling strategy can drive the expected leave-node-out error below
$60\%$ of the target variance.  Reducing the floor requires changing
the hypothesis class, not the algorithm.

\subsection{Open questions remaining}
\label{subsec:open}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*),leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Sharp matching constant beyond rate match.}  Upper and
    lower bounds match in rate ($1/\sqrt{N-n}$) after the transductive
    Rademacher upgrade of Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}.
    A multi-point Fano argument
    (\texttt{notes/04\_sharp\_transductive\_constant.tex}~\S 4)
    should tighten the constant from a factor $\approx 10$ to $\approx 4$.
    Cosmetic for the operational regime, important for the cleanest
    statement of the Cram\'er--Rao analogy.

\item \textbf{Importance-weighted transduction identity.}  Extending
    Lemma~\ref{lem:transduction} to non-uniform protocols (e.g.,
    leverage-score sampling for active learning) requires an
    importance-weighted variant of \eqref{eq:transduction-identity}.
    Mechanical, deferred to the C3 companion paper.

\item \textbf{Approximate ERM.}  Assumption~\ref{a:erm} requires strict
    ERM.  For approximate ERM with $\epsilon_T$-suboptimality, an
    additional $(\rho - 1) \E_T[\epsilon_T]$ slack term appears in
    Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-lower-bound}.  For regularised learners
    (ridge, GBM with early stopping), this slack is typically
    $O(d/n)$ --- small but non-zero.

\item \textbf{Misspecified $\Hcal_d$ at deployment.}  The bound applies
    to the actual reachable class $\Hcal_d$ of the deployed learner,
    not to the modeller's nominal description of it.  In practice,
    learners with implicit biases (e.g., neural networks trained by
    SGD, which empirically reach a small "minimum-norm" subspace of
    $\R^N$) have a $\Hcal_d$ smaller than the parametric capacity
    suggests; identifying the operative $\Hcal_d$ for such learners is
    an open empirical question.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}

The framework treats the target $\by$ as deterministic.  Extension to
noisy observations $y^{\mathrm{obs}}_v = y_v + \xi_v$ requires either
(a) absorbing the noise into the residual budget $(1 - \Rspec)\Var$,
which the bound then describes as an upper limit on the
\emph{signal-plus-noise}-explainable variance; or (b) a parallel
inverse-problem analysis along the lines of
\citet{BauerPereverzev2005,Cavalier2008}, which we have not pursued
because the deterministic target is the operative case in our motivating
applications (materials informatics, urban mobility, recommender systems).

The framework does not address \emph{model selection}: the choice of
$\Hcal_d$ itself is outside the bound's scope.  Selecting $\Hcal_d$ to
maximise $\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)$ subject to fixed Rademacher complexity
is a separate problem (essentially representation learning), on which
the bound only comments indirectly via the trade-off between
$\Rspec(\Hcal_d, \by)$ and $\mathcal{R}_n(\Hcal_d)$ in the slack of
Theorem~\ref{thm:universal-upper-bound}.
